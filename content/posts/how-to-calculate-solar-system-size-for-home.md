Your electricity bill is the single most important document in your whole solar project, and most people barely glance at it before calling a contractor.

I get it. You've probably spent the last few weeks getting quotes, reading Reddit threads, maybe watching a few YouTube videos where someone installs 40 panels on their barn roof. It's a lot. And somewhere in all of that noise, you're wondering: how many panels do I actually need? What size system makes sense for my house, my roof, my budget? You're not trying to become a solar engineer. You just don't want to spend $20,000 on the wrong system.

Here's what I tell people who come to me at this stage: sizing a solar system is mostly arithmetic, but the inputs matter enormously. Get the inputs wrong and you'll either overbuild (wasting money) or underbuild (still paying a painful electric bill every month). Let me walk you through the actual process.

## Start With Your Annual Kilowatt-Hour Usage

Pull up your utility account online and look for 12 months of usage data. Not just last month's bill. Twelve months, because your summer air conditioning load and your winter heating load are completely different animals.

Add up all 12 months of kilowatt-hours (kWh). This is your annual consumption. The average U.S. home uses about 10,500 kWh per year according to the EIA, but that number is almost meaningless for your situation. I've seen 1,400 square-foot homes in Phoenix using 22,000 kWh because they're running a pool pump and electric water heater. I've seen 2,800 square-foot homes in Oregon using 7,000 kWh because they're on natural gas for everything and run a tight ship on energy efficiency.

Your number is your number. Write it down.

Now think about what's coming. Are you planning to buy an EV in the next couple of years? EVs typically add 3,000 to 5,000 kWh per year depending on how much you drive. Are you replacing a gas furnace with a heat pump? That could add another 2,000 to 4,000 kWh annually. If any of that's on your horizon, add it in now. It's much cheaper to slightly oversize your system today than to add panels later (different racking, possible new permits, different inverter capacity).

This tripped me up the first time I worked through a system design. Peak sun hours aren't the total hours of daylight. They're a measure of solar irradiance: specifically, the number of hours per day when your location receives 1,000 watts of solar energy per square meter. It's a standardized way to compare locations.

San Diego gets roughly 5.5 peak sun hours per day. Seattle gets about 3.5. Somewhere in Massachusetts, you're looking at 4.0 to 4.2. These numbers come from the National Renewable Energy Laboratory's solar resource data, and you can look up your specific location using their PVWatts Calculator at pvwatts.nrel.gov. It's free, it's accurate, and I'd trust it over anything a solar salesperson tells you.

This number directly determines how much work your panels can do per day, which means it directly determines how many panels you need.

## The Actual Sizing Math (Not That Scary)

Here's the formula. You need to find your required system size in kilowatts (kW):

**System size (kW) = Annual kWh usage / (Peak sun hours x 365)**

Let's run a real example. Say your home uses 12,000 kWh per year and you're in Atlanta, which gets about 4.5 peak sun hours per day.

12,000 / (4.5 x 365) = 12,000 / 1,642.5 = **7.3 kW**

That's your raw system size. But panels aren't perfect, inverters lose some efficiency, there's wiring loss, temperature derating, shading you haven't fully accounted for. Real-world systems typically operate at 75% to 85% of their rated capacity. So apply an 80% efficiency factor:

7.3 kW / 0.80 = **9.1 kW system**

Now convert to panel count. If you're using a 400-watt panel (a pretty standard spec for Qcells Q.PEAK DUO panels or the REC Alpha series), you'd divide:

9,100 watts / 400 watts = **approximately 23 panels**

That's your ballpark. A good installer will refine this using actual shading analysis software (Aurora or Helioscope are the industry standards), roof orientation data, and your utility's specific net metering rules.

One thing people don't ask enough: what's your goal? If your goal is to offset 100% of your electric bill, size to your full consumption. If your goal is to reduce your bill by 70% and keep upfront costs lower, size to that. And if your utility has a cap on system size for net metering (some do, at 10 kW for residential), that becomes a hard ceiling you'll need to design around.

## Roof Matters More Than Most Calculators Admit

You can do all this math and come up with 24 panels, then walk outside and realize your usable south-facing roof space fits maybe 14. That's a real conversation to have early.

A standard 400-watt panel is roughly 22 square feet. Twenty-four panels need about 528 square feet of usable roof area. Not total roof area. Usable: south or west-facing (east is workable, north is generally not worth it in the Northern Hemisphere), with minimal shading from trees or chimneys, and enough structural clearance from roof edges and ridges to satisfy fire code setbacks.

In California, for instance, the setback requirements under Title 24 mandate 3-foot clear pathways on at least two sides of a residential array. Other jurisdictions have similar rules. Your permitting office can tell you exactly what applies to your project.

If your roof comes up short on square footage, you have a few options. Higher-efficiency panels like the SunPower Maxeon 6 (rated up to 440 watts) squeeze more production out of less space. Or you accept a smaller system and pair it with aggressive efficiency improvements elsewhere.

## The Battery Question Changes the Math

If you're adding a battery (a Tesla Powerwall 3, a Franklin WH10, an Enphase IQ Battery 5P), you're not just sizing for grid offset anymore. You're sizing for backup duration.

Here's what I tell people: figure out which loads you actually care about keeping on during a grid outage. Your refrigerator, some lights, phone charging, maybe your Wi-Fi router. That's probably 1.5 to 2.5 kWh per day of critical loads. A Powerwall 3 holds 13.5 kWh usable. In a serious outage, that might cover two to three days of critical loads without any solar recharging.

If you want your system to be genuinely self-sufficient for longer outages, you need to size your solar array to both cover your daily usage *and* recharge the battery each day. That means a larger array than a pure grid-tied system would require. Sometimes significantly larger. EnergySage's market data consistently shows that battery-paired systems run $10,000 to $15,000 more than equivalent grid-tied systems, so it's worth being honest with yourself about what you actually need backup for before committing to that cost.

## What a Responsible Quote Should Look Like

Once you've run your own numbers, you'll be in a much better position to evaluate what installers are telling you. And honestly, this is where sizing math earns its keep.

If you've calculated you need around a 9 kW system and an installer is quoting you a 6.5 kW system "because that's what'll offset 70% of your bill," ask them to show you the production estimate year by year. If another installer is proposing 14 kW without a compelling reason (like an EV you're definitely buying), that's worth questioning too. Oversizing isn't free money. You'll hit production limits on your inverter and your net metering agreement.

Ask every installer for a production estimate in kWh per year, not just system size in kW. Those numbers should be close to what PVWatts calculates independently. If they're wildly higher, someone is either using inflated peak sun hours or hasn't accounted for shading accurately.
