If you've been pricing out solar this spring, you're probably feeling a little whiplash. The 30% federal tax credit is gone, installers are quoting higher effective costs than anything you saw online a year ago, and yet everyone seems to be adding a battery now. What's actually going on? You're not imagining the shift. According to the SEIA/Wood Mackenzie Q2 2026 Solar Market Insight report, a record 45% of new residential solar installations in Q1 2026 were paired with battery storage, up from about 35% throughout 2025. That number has never been higher. And understanding why it jumped tells you a lot about whether storage makes sense for your situation right now.

## The Tax Credit Is Gone, and That Changes the Calculation in a Non-Obvious Way

Here's what I tell people who are surprised the solar-plus-storage combo is surging right after the incentive expired: the math didn't get worse for storage specifically. It got worse for everything equally.

The Section 25D 30% residential tax credit, which covered both solar panels and batteries installed alongside them, expired December 31, 2025 under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act. That's a real hit. On a $25,000 system, that's $7,500 you no longer get back at tax time. BloombergNEF projects U.S. residential solar additions will fall 15% in 2026 to just 4.1 GW, the lowest level in five years, and that drop is almost entirely attributable to the credit expiration.

But here's the thing. When the incentive was alive, some homeowners bought solar-only systems because the credit made even a modest setup pencil out. Now that the credit is gone, the people still moving forward are doing so because they have a stronger underlying reason: backup power, grid independence, time-of-use rate management. Those homeowners almost always want a battery. The credit's expiration didn't kill demand for storage. It filtered out the buyers who were on the fence about it.

Hardware prices also kept falling. The median installed price of solar dropped 14% year-over-year to $3.44 per watt in 2025, per Aurora Solar's 2026 Solar Snapshot. Storage costs have followed a similar curve. The credit covered 30% of a more expensive system. Today you're potentially paying full price on a cheaper system. The net cost difference isn't always as dramatic as the headline sounds.

You might be wondering how much of this battery trend is marketing-driven anxiety versus real need. It's a fair question, and the honest answer is: it's mostly real.

Aurora Solar's 2026 Solar Snapshot surveyed homeowners across the country and found that 53% say the power grid has become less reliable in their area, and 62% say extreme weather is affecting their region. Those aren't abstract concerns. If you've sat through a 90-degree August without power for three days because a substation got overwhelmed, or watched your sump pump die during a nor'easter while your neighbors with Tesla Powerwalls went about their evenings, your interest in backup power isn't irrational.

The geographic variation matters here though. If you're in southern California, Florida, Texas, or anywhere with regular hurricane or wildfire exposure, the resilience case for storage is genuinely strong. If you're in a mild climate with a reliable utility and no time-of-use rates, the payback math is harder to make work. I always push people to pull their actual outage history from their utility's website before making this call. Most utilities publish it and most homeowners have never looked.

## What the Installer Trend Actually Tells You

When nearly one-third of installers expect more than 75% of their 2026 projects to include storage, per Aurora Solar survey data from May 2026, that's a meaningful industry signal. Installers see what converts, what customers regret, and what generates callbacks. They're not pushing batteries purely because margins are better, though that's sometimes true. They're seeing more homeowners come in already decided on storage before the first site visit.

Only 3% of solar-engaged homeowners say they don't want a battery, according to that same Aurora data. That's a remarkable number. It suggests the default assumption has flipped. A few years ago, storage was the upsell. Now, solar-only is what requires an explanation.

Here's what I tell people about that shift: don't let the trend pressure you into a battery you don't need, but don't dismiss it as hype either. If your utility has time-of-use rates where peak hours cost two or three times off-peak rates, a battery can pay back in stored arbitrage. If your state has eliminated net metering or dramatically cut the export credit, you have a financial reason to self-consume more of what you generate, and a battery helps you do that. If neither applies to you yet, check your utility's rate case filings. Utility commissions in more than a dozen states have pending proceedings that could change export compensation within the next two to three years.

## The Red Flags to Watch When a Contractor Quotes You Storage

Adding a battery to a solar project introduces complexity that not every installer handles well. A few things to verify before you sign:

The battery should be properly sized to your critical loads, not just the largest unit the manufacturer offers. Ask the contractor to show you the load calculation. If they can't, that's a problem. A 13.5 kWh Powerwall 3 is meaningless sizing guidance if you haven't identified what you actually want to run during an outage.

Make sure the quote separates the solar cost from the storage cost clearly. Some installers bundle them in a way that obscures the effective price per watt and per kilowatt-hour. You want to be able to compare bids line by line.

Ask specifically whether the system is configured for whole-home backup or partial-home backup. Most residential batteries do partial backup unless you've sized up and added a sub-panel or automatic transfer switch. Either approach is fine, but you need to know what you're buying.

Finally, confirm the battery qualifies for your state's storage incentive, if one exists. California's SGIP program, Massachusetts' ConnectedSolutions, New York's Con Ed Smart Charge program, and several others can meaningfully change your net cost even without the federal credit. These programs have their own eligibility windows and capacity limits, so timing matters.

The convergence happening right now is real and it's driving a genuine shift in how homeowners approach solar. The people still moving forward without the federal credit are doing so with clearer eyes about what they're buying and why. If you're in that group, storage deserves a serious look, not because everyone's doing it, but because the hardware is cheaper, the grid is less dependable, and the financial case has gotten genuinely stronger in the last 18 months.
