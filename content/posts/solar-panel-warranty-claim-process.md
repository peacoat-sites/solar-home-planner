A homeowner I worked with last year had a Hanwha Q CELLS panel that cracked along the back sheet, no storm, no impact, just a hot Arizona summer doing what Arizona summers do. The panel was four years old, well within the 12-year product warranty. She had her original purchase invoice, her installation records, a timestamped photo from the day she noticed it, and the installer's business card. Should have been a clean claim. Instead she spent six weeks trading emails with a warranty department that kept asking for documents she'd already sent, then tried to tell her the crack was "physical damage" (excluded) rather than a manufacturing defect. She got it resolved, but only because she knew what she was entitled to and pushed back with specific language from the warranty document itself.

That experience is pretty representative of how solar panel warranty claims actually go. The warranty is real. The coverage is real. Getting it honored without turning it into a part-time job requires knowing the process before you need it.

## What You're Actually Covered For (And What You're Not)

| Warranty Type | Coverage | Typical Duration | What It Covers |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Product (Materials/Equipment) | Physical defects in panel | 10-25 years | Delamination, cracked back sheets, cell corrosion, frame separation, junction box failures |
| Performance | Output guarantee | 25 years | Panel produces ≥80-87% of rated output at 25-year mark |

Most residential panels come with two separate warranties, and conflating them is the first mistake most homeowners make.

The **product warranty** (also called the materials or equipment warranty) covers physical defects in the panel itself: delamination, [cracked back sheets](/solar-panel-cracked-what-to-do/), cell corrosion, frame separation, junction box failures. Hanwha Q CELLS currently offers 12 years on most of their residential line. SunPower's Maxeon panels carry 25 years on the product side. LG, before they exited the solar business, was at 25 years too. Most other tier-one brands sit between 10 and 15 years.

The **performance warranty** is different. It guarantees the panel will produce at least a specified percentage of its rated output over time, typically 80 to 87 percent at the 25-year mark. If your panels are measurably underproducing relative to that curve, you have a legitimate performance claim. These are harder to prove because you have to account for shading, soiling, inverter losses, and seasonal variation before you can isolate genuine panel degradation.

What most people don't realize is that the warranty you're reading is from the manufacturer, not your installer. If your installer goes out of business (which happens constantly in this industry), you still have a direct claim pathway to the manufacturer. You might need to hire a licensed electrician to do the diagnostic work the installer would have done, but the coverage itself doesn't disappear with the company.

A few things that genuinely void coverage, depending on the manufacturer: improper installation (wrong racking, too much mechanical stress on the frame), damage from walking on the panels, pressure washing, modifications to the junction box, or connecting the panels to equipment outside their rated specs. Read those exclusions. They're usually in section 4 or 5 of the warranty document, and they're specific.

When your system is installed, you should have: a signed copy of the installation contract, the manufacturer warranty certificates for the panels and inverter (these are usually separate documents), your building permit and final inspection sign-off, photos of the completed installation from multiple angles, the monitoring system baseline data from the first 60 days, and the installer's ROC or contractor license number.

The homeowner who wins her warranty claim quickly is the one who can send a single organized PDF with every document the manufacturer will eventually ask for. Send it on the first contact. It removes their ability to slow-roll you with repeated requests.

## The Actual Claim Process, Step by Step

Once you've identified a potential defect, here's what a methodical claim process looks like:

**Document the problem first.** Photograph everything with your phone, which embeds date and GPS metadata in the image file. Don't clean the panel before photographing it. If you have a [thermal imaging camera or can rent one](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=thermal+imaging+camera+for+solar+panels&tag=contentportfo-20) (the site may earn a commission), a thermal scan showing a hot spot or dead cell is stronger evidence than a visual photo alone.

**Check your monitoring data.** Log in to your SolarEdge, Enphase, or Fronius monitoring portal and download the production data for that panel or string going back at least 90 days. A sudden drop in a specific panel's output is strong evidence of a failure. Gradual underperformance across all panels is more likely soiling or shading.

**Contact your installer first.** Even if you suspect the manufacturer is responsible, the installer is often your fastest path because they have direct relationships with manufacturer reps. Give them 5 to 7 business days to respond. If they're non-responsive or out of business, go directly to the manufacturer's warranty support line.

**File the claim in writing.** Always. Phone calls don't create records. Send an email or use the manufacturer's online warranty portal, and keep the confirmation. In your opening message, include your name, address, system installation date, the specific panel model and serial number (usually printed on a sticker on the back of the panel or on your installation documentation), a description of the defect, and the attached photos and production data.

**Expect a site visit request.** Most manufacturers will want an authorized technician to inspect the panel before approving a claim. This might be your original installer, a manufacturer-designated service provider, or in some cases a third-party inspector they hire. You're generally not paying for this, but confirm that in writing before scheduling.

**Be specific about what you're requesting.** A replacement panel? A prorated refund? Reimbursement for the labor to replace it? Labor coverage varies significantly by manufacturer, and it's often negotiated. SunPower and REC Group tend to be better about including labor costs in claims. Others will replace the panel and leave you to find someone to install it.

If the claim is denied and you believe it shouldn't be, request the denial in writing with the specific exclusion clause they're citing. Then respond point by point. That's exactly what my Arizona homeowner did, and it worked.

## The Contractor Red Flags That Kill Claims Later

I've seen plenty of installations that were technically functional but set the homeowner up for warranty trouble down the road. A few patterns:

Installers who don't register your panels with the manufacturer. Some warranties require product registration within 30 to 90 days of installation to get the full coverage period. If your installer skipped this step, you might be looking at a shorter coverage window without knowing it. Check directly with the manufacturer using your serial numbers.

Panels installed without a permit. If your system didn't go through the building department and get a final inspection, a manufacturer can argue the installation was improper. That's a real exclusion in most warranty documents. Permitted work is documented, inspected, and defensible.

No-name or overly discounted panels from an installer who's pushing you toward something other than the top tier brands. According to [EnergySage's market data](https://news.energysage.com/), the most commonly installed brands in U.S. residential projects are Q CELLS, REC, Canadian Solar, SunPower, and Jinko. Their warranty support infrastructure exists and functions. Some lower-tier manufacturers have warranty terms on paper that they can't actually support because they don't have U.S.-based service operations.


---

The warranty paperwork you shove in a drawer when the installers leave is only as useful as your ability to act on it when something goes wrong. Keep your documents organized, register your panels, and don't accept a claim denial without asking exactly which exclusion applies to your situation. Most legitimate defects should be covered. Getting them covered just takes knowing the system well enough to work it.
