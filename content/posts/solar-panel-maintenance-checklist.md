Most homeowners go three or four years without touching their solar panels, then wonder why their production numbers have drifted down 12% and their installer won't return calls. I've seen it more times than I can count.

The good news is that solar panels are genuinely low-maintenance. The bad news is that "low maintenance" has been used as a sales tool so often that people mistake it for "no maintenance," and that's where the money starts quietly disappearing.

Here's what an honest maintenance checklist looks like, the kind you'd actually use rather than skim and forget.

---

## Start With Your Production Data, Not Your Roof

Before you grab a ladder or a hose, the most important thing you can do is pull up your inverter's monitoring app and look at your historical production numbers. I mean actually look at them, month over month, same billing period last year versus this year.

Most modern inverters (SolarEdge, Enphase, Fronius, SMA) have free monitoring portals that show you panel-level or string-level output. If you have microinverters or power optimizers, you can see exactly which panel is underperforming. This is where your real maintenance checklist begins, not up on the roof.

A single panel dropping 20-30% while its neighbors are fine is almost never a dirt problem. It usually points to a failing microinverter, a shading issue you hadn't noticed (a tree that's grown, a neighbor's new fence), or a potential-induced degradation situation on older panels. Dirt degrades your whole array more or less evenly. When one panel is the outlier, something else is going on.

What you're looking for in the data: a gradual, system-wide decline of more than 10-15% compared to the same season last year, after you've accounted for any unusual weather. That's the signal to get on the roof and start looking.

Twice a year is the right cadence for most climates. I do mine in late spring (after pollen season wraps up) and again in late fall before the winter sets in. If you live somewhere with heavy bird activity, a lot of dust, or pine trees overhead, quarterly is not overkill.

**Dirt, dust, and organic debris.** Bird droppings are the big one. A single bird dropping sitting on a cell can cause a phenomenon called "hot spotting" where that cell gets dramatically hotter than the rest of the panel and can eventually damage the cell permanently. Regular dust reduces output, but it's a lot more forgiving. Pollen can be surprisingly stubborn after rain because it bonds with moisture and bakes in the sun. If your panels look yellow-green in spring, that's pollen, and it won't rinse off as easily as you'd hope.

**Panel surface condition.** Get close and look for microcracks, discoloration (particularly a brownish yellowing, called delamination), or any bubbling in the encapsulant. These are warranty issues, not cleaning issues. Document them with photos and contact your installer or the panel manufacturer directly. LG's solar panel warranty, for example, was a nightmare situation for some customers when LG exited the solar market in 2022, so if you have LG Neon panels, know that Hanwha Q CELLS is now handling some of those warranty claims.

**Racking and mounting hardware.** Look for loose bolts, rust on any uncoated steel components, and cracked or shifted standoffs. In high-wind areas this matters more than people realize. A panel that's shifted even slightly can create a leak point in your roof.

**Wiring and conduit.** You're mostly looking for critters. Squirrels, in particular, will absolutely destroy wiring. I had a reader email me last year after a squirrel chewed through three homerun cables on a 10kW system and caused an arc fault. The repair was $900. Mesh critter guards that run along the bottom edge of panels are cheap, maybe $40-80 for a typical roof, and worth every penny.

**Inverter and electrical components.** Check your inverter display or indicator lights monthly. A solid green light on a string inverter means it's operating. Any amber or red fault codes, look them up immediately in the manual. Don't wait. Also check that your disconnect switches aren't corroded and that the conduit hasn't been disturbed.

## Cleaning: The Part Everyone Overthinks

Here's my honest take: in most of the country, rain does about 80% of the work for you. If you're in a low-tilt area (panels under 10 degrees of pitch), a dry climate like Phoenix or Albuquerque, or you're under a lot of trees, you'll need to supplement. Otherwise, a good rain and you're mostly fine.

When you do clean, the method matters. Use plain deionized or distilled water when possible. Tap water leaves mineral deposits (those white streaks you see) that can accumulate over time. A soft brush or a squeegee with a long handle is better than a pressure washer. Never use a pressure washer on solar panels. The water can force its way into junction boxes and frame seals, and you can crack cells with the pressure if you're too close. I've seen warranty claims denied specifically because of pressure washer damage.

There are decent purpose-built cleaning kits worth having if you plan to do this yourself. Morning is the best time to clean. Panels are cool, which matters because spraying cold water on hot glass can cause thermal shock and micro-cracking. Early morning, the surface hasn't had time to heat up, and you'll also see a production boost during a part of the day when the sun is actively working.

One thing most people don't realize: cleaning frequency has diminishing returns pretty fast. EnergySage's market data on solar performance suggests that in most U.S. climates, soiling losses average 1-7% annually, and you can recover most of that with one or two targeted cleanings per year. You don't need to be out there every month with a brush.

A [home energy monitor](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=home+energy+monitor&tag=your-affiliate-tag) like the Emporia Vue 2 (around $70) or Sense (around $299) installed at your main panel gives you whole-home visibility into what your solar is producing versus what you're actually consuming. More specifically: get in the habit of checking your monitoring app every week or two, not just when you're worried something is wrong. Production problems creep up slowly and most people only notice them when they get a surprisingly high electric bill six months later. By then, they've lost potentially hundreds of dollars in generation they should have been banking.

The U.S. Department of Energy's homeowner solar guide makes this point well, noting that consistent monitoring is one of the highest-return activities a solar homeowner can do, and it costs nothing beyond the few minutes it takes. Set a recurring calendar reminder. Monthly is fine. Weekly if you're type-A about it.

## When to Call a Professional

Most of what I've described above is genuinely DIY-friendly. But there are a few situations where you need someone with the right licensing and equipment:

Anything involving your electrical connections at the inverter or combiner box should be handled by a licensed electrician or solar technician. Same goes for any actual panel replacements. If you suspect an arc fault or have tripped a rapid shutdown system, do not just reset it and move on. That's a fire risk.

If your system is more than 10 years old, it's worth paying for a thermographic inspection (sometimes called a thermal imaging inspection). A technician uses an infrared camera to identify hot spots, failing bypass diodes, and early signs of delamination that aren't visible to the naked eye. Expect to pay $150-400 depending on your system size and who you hire. It's not something you need every year, but at the 10-year mark and again around year 15, it's money well spent.

Roof penetrations and flashing should be checked by a roofer during your next roof inspection, not just by your solar installer. I've seen installations where the flashing was done beautifully from a solar standpoint but left gaps a roofer would never have approved. After major hailstorms or high wind events, get a roofer up there along with your solar inspection.

## FAQ

### How often should I clean my solar panels?

For most of the U.S., one to two cleanings per year is enough. If you're in a dry, dusty climate or have significant tree coverage and bird activity, quarterly cleaning makes sense. Rain handles a lot of it, but it won't clear heavy pollen or bird droppings on its own.

### Do solar panels need maintenance in winter?

Yes, but it's mostly about snow removal and monitoring. Light snow will slide off on its own from most pitched roofs. Heavy accumulated snow can reduce production to zero and adds weight, so use a soft snow rake with a foam head (not a metal one) to clear it. Don't use salt or hot water.

### How do I know if a solar panel is failing?

Consistent underproduction on a single panel relative to others in your array is the clearest sign, visible only if you have panel-level monitoring like Enphase or SolarEdge. Visible cracks, discoloration, or bubbling in the panel surface are also indicators. A thermal imaging inspection can catch issues that aren't visible at all.

### Can I walk on my solar panels to clean them?

No. Solar panels are not designed to be walked on. Even panels that are marketed as walkable have strict weight distribution requirements. Walking on them risks cracking cells and voiding your warranty. Use an extension pole from the roof edge or a ladder positioned beside the panels.

### What does my solar warranty actually cover for maintenance issues?

Most panel warranties are split into two parts: a product warranty (typically 10-25 years covering defects in materials and workmanship) and a performance warranty (usually guaranteeing 80-90% output after 25 years). Maintenance-related damage from improper cleaning or physical impact is almost always excluded. Read your specific warranty document. And keep records of every inspection and cleaning you do, because if you ever need to file a claim, documentation is what makes it stick.

---

The honest summary of all this: most of your maintenance is really just paying attention. Check your numbers regularly, clean when you need to, get eyes on the hardware twice a year, and don't ignore warning signs because fixing a small problem in year three costs a lot less than replacing a panel in year seven. Your system is probably fine. But "probably" is something you should be able to prove with data, not just assume.
