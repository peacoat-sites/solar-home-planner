Most homeowners go three or four years without touching their solar panels, then wonder why their production numbers have drifted down 12% and their installer won't return calls. I've seen it more times than I can count.

The good news is that solar panels are genuinely low-maintenance. The bad news is that "low maintenance" has been used as a sales tool so often that people mistake it for "no maintenance," and that's where the money starts quietly disappearing.

Here's what an honest maintenance checklist looks like, the kind you'd actually use rather than skim and forget.

## Start With Your Production Data, Not Your Roof

Before you grab a ladder or a hose, pull up your inverter's monitoring app and look at your historical production numbers. I mean actually look at them, month over month, same billing period last year versus this year.

Most modern inverters (SolarEdge, Enphase, Fronius, SMA) have free monitoring portals that show you panel-level or string-level output. If you have microinverters or power optimizers, you can see exactly which panel is underperforming. This is where your real maintenance starts, not up on the roof.

A single panel dropping 20-30% while its neighbors are fine? Almost never dirt. It usually points to a failing microinverter, a shading issue you hadn't noticed (a tree that's grown, a neighbor's new fence), or potential-induced degradation on older panels. Dirt degrades your whole array more or less evenly. When one panel's the outlier, something else is happening.

What you're hunting for in the data: a gradual, system-wide decline of more than 10-15% compared to the same season last year, after accounting for any unusual weather. That's your signal to get on the roof.

## The Physical Inspection: What to Actually Check and When

| Issue | Inspection Frequency | Severity | Action Required |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Dirt, dust, organic debris | Twice yearly (quarterly in heavy bird/tree areas) | Low to medium | Clean with soft brush and distilled water |
| Bird droppings | Ongoing visual checks | High | Remove immediately to prevent hot spotting |
| Microcracks or delamination | Twice yearly | High | Document with photos; contact installer or manufacturer |
| Loose bolts or rust on racking | Twice yearly | Medium | Tighten hardware; monitor for corrosion |
| Critter damage to wiring | Monthly visual checks | High | Install mesh critter guards ($40-80) |
| Inverter fault codes | Monthly | High | Check manual immediately; do not delay |
| System-wide output decline | Monthly (review data) | High | Investigate if >10-15% drop year-over-year |
| Single panel underperformance | Monthly (review data) | High | Check for failing inverter, shading, or degradation |

Twice a year is the right cadence for most climates. I do mine in late spring (after pollen season wraps up) and again in late fall before the winter sets in. Heavy bird activity, lots of dust, or pine trees overhead? Go quarterly.

**Dirt, dust, and organic debris.** Bird droppings are the culprit. A single dropping sitting on a cell causes something called "[hot spotting](/solar-panel-hot-spot-problem-fix/)" where that cell gets dramatically hotter than the rest of the panel and can eventually damage it permanently. Regular dust reduces output, but it's forgiving. Pollen, though, is stubborn after rain because it bonds with moisture and bakes in the sun. If your panels look yellow-green in spring, that's pollen, and it won't rinse off as easily as you'd hope.

**Panel surface condition.** Get close and look for microcracks, discoloration (particularly a brownish yellowing called delamination), or any bubbling in the encapsulant. These are warranty issues, not cleaning issues. Document them with photos and contact your installer or the panel manufacturer. LG's solar panel warranty was a nightmare when LG exited the solar market in 2022, so if you have LG Neon panels, know that Hanwha Q CELLS is now handling some of those claims.

**Racking and mounting hardware.** Look for loose bolts, rust on any uncoated steel, and cracked or shifted standoffs. In high-wind areas this matters way more than people realize. A panel shifted even slightly can create a leak point in your roof.

**Wiring and conduit.** You're mostly looking for critters. Squirrels will absolutely destroy wiring. A reader emailed me last year after a squirrel chewed through three homerun cables on a 10kW system and caused an arc fault. The repair was $900. [Mesh critter guards](/solar-bird-proofing-and-critter-guard/) that run along the bottom edge of panels cost maybe $40-80 for a typical roof. Worth every penny.

**Inverter and electrical components.** Check your inverter display or indicator lights monthly. A solid green light on a string inverter means it's operating fine. Any amber or red fault codes? Look them up immediately in the manual. Don't wait. Also check that your disconnect switches aren't corroded and the conduit hasn't been disturbed.

## Cleaning: The Part Everyone Overthinks

In most of the country, rain does about 80% of the work for you. If you're in a low-tilt area (panels under 10 degrees of pitch), a dry climate like Phoenix or Albuquerque, or you're under a lot of trees, you'll need to supplement. Otherwise, a good rain and you're mostly fine.

When you do clean, method matters. Use plain deionized or distilled water when possible. Tap water leaves mineral deposits (those white streaks) that accumulate over time. A soft brush or squeegee with a long handle beats a pressure washer every single time. Never use a pressure washer on solar panels. Water can force its way into junction boxes and frame seals, and you can crack cells if you're too close. I've seen warranty claims denied specifically because of pressure washer damage.

There are solid purpose-built cleaning kits worth having if you plan to DIY. [A solar panel cleaning kit](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=solar+panel+cleaning+kit&tag=contentportfo-20) with an extension pole and soft-bristle brush runs about $30-60 on Amazon and makes the job way safer since you can reach panels from the edge of the roof or a ladder without stepping on them. (Full disclosure: the site may earn a small commission on that link.)

Clean in the morning. Panels are cool, which matters because spraying cold water on hot glass can cause thermal shock and micro-cracking. Early morning means the surface hasn't had time to heat up, and you'll also see a production boost during a part of the day when the sun's actually working.

Here's the thing most people miss: cleaning frequency has diminishing returns. EnergySage's market data on solar performance shows that in most U.S. climates, soiling losses average 1-7% annually, and you recover most of that with one or two targeted cleanings per year. You don't need to be out there every month with a brush.

## The Monitoring Habit That Saves You Real Money

A [home energy monitor](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=home+energy+monitor&tag=contentportfo-20) like the Emporia Vue 2 (around $70) or Sense (around $299) installed at your main panel gives you whole-home visibility into what your solar produces versus what you're consuming. It's genuinely useful, not just a gadget. (Again, the site may earn a commission on purchases through that link.)

Get in the habit of checking your monitoring app every week or two, not just when you're worried something's wrong. Production problems creep up slowly and most people only notice them when they get a surprisingly high electric bill six months later. By then, they've lost potentially hundreds of dollars in generation.

The U.S. Department of Energy's homeowner solar guide makes this point well: consistent monitoring is one of the highest-return activities a solar homeowner can do, and it costs nothing beyond a few minutes. Set a recurring calendar reminder. Monthly is fine. Weekly if you're type-A about it.

## When to Call a Professional

Most of what I've described is genuinely DIY-friendly. But some situations need someone with the right licensing and equipment:

Anything involving your electrical connections at the inverter or combiner box gets handled by a licensed electrician or solar technician. Same with panel replacements. If you suspect an arc fault or have tripped a rapid shutdown system, do not just reset it and move on. That's a fire risk.

For systems more than 10 years old, a thermographic inspection is worth the cost. A technician uses an infrared camera to spot hot spots, failing bypass diodes, and early delamination signs you can't see with your eyes. Expect $150-400 depending on your system size and who you hire. You don't need it every year, but at the 10-year mark and again around year 15, it's money well spent.

Roof penetrations and flashing should be checked by a roofer during your next roof inspection, not just by your solar installer. I've seen installations where the flashing looked beautiful from a solar standpoint but left gaps a roofer would never approve. After major hailstorms or high wind events, get a roofer up there along with your solar inspection.

---

The honest summary: most of your maintenance is really just paying attention. Check your numbers regularly, clean when you need to, get eyes on the hardware twice a year, and don't ignore warning signs because fixing a small problem in year three costs way less than replacing a panel in year seven. Your system is probably fine. But "probably" is something you should be able to prove with data, not just assume.
