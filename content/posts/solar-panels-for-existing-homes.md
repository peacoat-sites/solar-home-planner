Your electric bill arrives in July, and it's $340. You've got central AC, a couple teenagers, maybe a home office running all day. You look at the neighbors' roof, see those panels, and think: *could I actually do that?* Yes. But the gap between "thinking about solar" and "having a system that actually saves you money" is filled with decisions most installers won't walk you through honestly. Let's fix that.

## Is Your Existing Home Actually a Good Candidate?

This is the question nobody wants to answer directly, because the honest answer is sometimes "not really." So let's be blunt about what matters.

**Roof condition first.** If your roof is more than 15 years old, you need to deal with that before you go solar. Mounting a 25-year system on a roof that needs replacement in five years means paying to remove and reinstall the panels later. That can run $3,000 to $6,000 depending on your system size and who does the work. Get a roofer out there first. If you're within a few years of replacement, do it now and coordinate with your solar installer. Many will time the work together.

**Roof orientation and shading are the big technical factors.** A south-facing roof pitch between 15 and 40 degrees is the sweet spot in most of the U.S. East and west-facing roofs still work, typically producing 10 to 20 percent less than south-facing. Flat roofs work too, with ballasted racking systems that tilt the panels to the right angle. What kills production isn't orientation, it's shading. A single tree branch casting a shadow on one panel can drag down your whole string if the system isn't designed with optimizers or microinverters. Have your installer run a shading analysis using actual software, not just a visual inspection.

**Your electrical panel needs attention.** Most homes built before the mid-2000s have a 100-amp or 150-amp service panel. A standard residential solar installation usually requires a 200-amp panel to safely accommodate the interconnection. Upgrading the panel can add $1,500 to $3,500 to your project. Some installers gloss over this or quote you a system price that excludes the panel upgrade. Ask upfront: does your quote include any necessary electrical work?

Bigger isn't always better. The goal is to match your system output to your actual consumption, not to slap as many panels as possible on your roof.

Pull your last 12 months of electric bills and add up your total kilowatt-hours used. Say you used 14,400 kWh over the year, which is around the U.S. residential average. Divide that by 365 to get your daily usage: roughly 39 kWh per day.

Now apply a production factor. NREL maintains solar resource data across the entire country through their PVWatts calculator, which lets you plug in your location, system size, roof tilt, and orientation to get a realistic annual production estimate. In Phoenix, a 1 kW system facing south might produce around 1,800 kWh per year. In Seattle, that same 1 kW system might produce 1,000 kWh. These aren't guesses, they're calculated from decades of weather data.

For our 14,400 kWh household in the mid-Atlantic region (let's say roughly 1,300 kWh per kW per year), you'd need about an 11 kW system to cover your full usage. At current panel efficiencies, an 11 kW system takes up roughly 550 to 650 square feet of roof space.

A few things to factor in before you lock a number:

- If you're planning to buy an EV in the next few years, size up. Charging an EV at home can add 3,000 to 5,000 kWh annually to your usage.
- If your utility has a favorable net metering policy, going slightly oversized makes sense. If they offer poor compensation for exported power, size to match consumption more precisely.
- Battery storage changes the math. If you're adding a system like the Tesla Powerwall or Enphase IQ Battery, you can optimize for self-consumption rather than grid export.

## The Permit and Utility Interconnection Reality

I've seen homeowners get blindsided by this, and it's one of the biggest sources of project delays. Permits are not optional, and the timeline varies wildly by jurisdiction.

Most cities and counties require a building permit and an electrical permit for a solar installation. Some also require a separate structural permit if racking requires specific roof penetrations. The permit process typically takes one to three weeks in straightforward jurisdictions. In some places in California or the Northeast, I've seen it run six to twelve weeks.

Your utility also needs to approve the interconnection before you turn the system on. This is called the Interconnection Agreement. Without it, you can't legally export power to the grid, and in most states, your installer isn't allowed to energize the system. Utility review timelines range from a few days to several months depending on the utility and grid capacity in your area.

Here's what this means practically: when a contractor tells you "we can have your system installed and running in three weeks," they're often talking about physical installation only. The time from signing a contract to a fully operational system is more realistically two to four months when you include design, permitting, installation, and utility approval.

If you're in an HOA, check the CC&Rs before you do anything else. Federal law (the Solar Rights Act and various state-level solar access laws) limits HOAs from banning solar outright in most states, but they can still impose aesthetic requirements around panel placement and visibility. Don't assume your HOA can't cause problems. Document everything in writing.

## Comparing Quotes: What to Look For and What's a Red Flag

EnergySage's market data consistently shows that homeowners who get three or more quotes save an average of 20 percent compared to those who go with the first installer they talk to. That's real money on a $25,000 to $40,000 purchase.

Here's a quick comparison of what a solid quote versus a concerning one looks like:

| What to Check | Good Sign | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| Production estimate | Based on actual shading analysis and PVWatts data | Quoted as a round number with no methodology |
| Equipment specs | Named panel brand, model, wattage, and inverter type | "High-quality panels" with no specifications |
| Panel upgrade | Included in quote or explicitly broken out | Not mentioned at all |
| Warranty terms | 25-year product + 25-year production warranty on panels | Only mentions installation warranty |
| Timeline | Realistic 2-4 month estimate to full operation | "Done in two weeks" |
| Contract terms | Clear cancellation window (look for 3+ days) | High-pressure same-day signing bonus |
| Financing | Explains loan terms, interest rate, and true cost | Focuses only on monthly payment |

On financing: a solar loan that stretches over 20 or 25 years at 6 to 8 percent interest can mean you pay nearly double the system's cash price over the life of the loan. If cash purchase isn't an option, a 10 to 12 year term at a competitive rate is usually a better deal. Run the numbers yourself, not just the installer's payback estimate.

The federal Investment Tax Credit (ITC) currently sits at 30 percent of your total system cost. On a $30,000 system, that's $9,000 back on your taxes. But it's a tax credit, not a refund. You need to owe at least that much in federal taxes in the year you claim it, or carry the remainder forward. If your tax liability is typically under $4,000 a year, talk to a CPA before assuming you'll capture the full credit.

## After Installation: Monitoring, Maintenance, and Realistic Expectations

Once the system is running, the work isn't completely over. You should be monitoring production from day one.

Every major inverter manufacturer, Enphase, SolarEdge, SMA, offers a monitoring portal or app that shows your daily and lifetime production. Check it during the first month. You're looking for consistent output that tracks with sunny and cloudy days. If production seems low for the weather you're having, investigate. Don't wait six months and wonder why your bill hasn't changed.

Panel cleaning is less urgent than installers sometimes make it sound. In most climates, rain does a reasonable job. In dry, dusty regions like Southern California, Arizona, or Texas, dust and bird droppings can reduce output by 5 to 15 percent if left unchecked for months. A [basic solar panel cleaning kit](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=solar+panel+cleaning+kit&tag=contentportfo-20) (the site may earn a commission on purchases) makes this a straightforward once or twice a year task if you're comfortable on your roof. Use deionized or distilled water to avoid mineral deposits.

A [home energy monitor](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=home+energy+monitor&tag=contentportfo-20) like the Emporia Vue or Sense adds another layer of visibility (the site may earn a commission on purchases). These devices show real-time consumption by circuit, so you can see whether you're actually self-consuming your solar production or exporting most of it and importing again at peak rates. That data is incredibly useful for shifting usage habits, like running the dishwasher or EV charger during peak solar production hours.

Maintenance costs are low. The biggest expense in the system's life is typically inverter replacement, usually around year 12 to 15 for string inverters. Budget $1,500 to $2,500 for that. Microinverters, which sit on each panel individually, typically carry a 25-year warranty and sidestep this issue.
