You get three quotes for a rooftop solar system. Two companies seem nearly identical on paper: same panel brand, similar system size, prices within $800 of each other. Then you dig into their online reviews and realize one installer has a pattern of complaints about permits being pulled six months late and customer service that vanishes after the contract is signed. That $800 difference suddenly looks like a bargain for the other guy. Reading solar installer reviews isn't like reading Yelp reviews for a pizza place. The stakes are higher, the projects are more complex, and the red flags are buried in language most homeowners don't know to look for.

## Why Most Solar Reviews Are Useless (And How to Find the Ones That Aren't)

Here's the uncomfortable truth: a 4.8-star rating on Google means almost nothing in the solar industry without context. Companies actively solicit reviews immediately after installation, before any real problems have had time to surface. A leaky roof penetration might not show up until the first heavy rain. Inverter issues can take months to develop. And customers who financed their systems often don't realize they've been overcharged until they start comparing notes with neighbors.

The reviews that actually matter are the ones written 12 to 24 months after installation. Look for reviewers who mention their system's actual production numbers versus what was promised, whether the company handled warranty claims promptly, and what happened when something went wrong. A company that only has glowing reviews from the first 60 days of installation and nothing substantive after that is a company worth questioning.

Also look at how the company responds to negative reviews. A defensive, blame-shifting response to a one-star complaint tells you a lot about how they'll treat you when you call with a problem after your system is live. A good contractor owns the issue, explains what happened, and describes the resolution. That response is public, and it's a window into company culture.

Some of the most damning review content isn't dramatic. It's quiet. Watch for these specific patterns across multiple reviews:

**Permit and interconnection delays.** If more than two or three reviewers mention waiting four, five, or six months to get their system turned on, that's not bad luck. That's a process problem. A good installer handles utility interconnection paperwork constantly and knows exactly what each local utility requires. In my experience, systems that stall in the permit phase are almost always stalled because the installer submitted incomplete paperwork or used an inexperienced office staff member to manage the filings.

**Subcontractor surprises.** Some installers sell the job and then hand it off to a subcontractor you've never met. Reviewers who mention "a different crew than who we met with" or "nobody from the company was on-site" are often describing this situation. Subcontracting isn't automatically bad, but if the company never disclosed it and reviews show a pattern of this happening, ask directly before you sign anything.

**Production shortfalls without explanation.** A reviewer who says "my system produces about 30% less than they promised and nobody will return my calls" is describing a worst-case scenario you want to avoid. Look for whether the company offered any explanation or recalculation based on actual roof shading, panel orientation, or weather data.

**Financing confusion.** Solar loans and leases are complicated, and some installers obscure the full cost of a loan by quoting only the monthly payment. Reviewers who felt misled about loan terms, escalator clauses in PPAs, or the actual payback period are waving a flag you should take seriously.

## What Good Reviews Actually Look Like

Useful positive reviews are specific. "Great company, highly recommend" tells you nothing. But "They submitted our permit within a week, the crew was here when they said they'd be, and our system hit 103% of the projected output in the first year" tells you a lot.

Here's what I tell homeowners to look for in credible positive reviews:

- Mentions of specific timelines: permit submission, installation date, utility interconnection date
- A system size (in kW) and a real production number or comparison to the utility estimate
- Comments about how the company handled a specific problem, even a small one
- Reviewer mentions they're writing the review 6+ months after installation

[EnergySage's market data](https://news.energysage.com/) consistently shows that installer quality, not panel brand, is the single biggest predictor of customer satisfaction. An average panel installed by a meticulous crew will outperform a premium panel installed carelessly every single time. The reviews that reflect genuine craftsmanship tend to read differently than scripted five-star blurbs.

## Where to Actually Read the Reviews

Don't stop at Google. Here's a practical cross-reference checklist:

| Platform | What it's good for | What to watch for |
|---|---|---|
| Google Reviews | Volume, general sentiment, company responses | Recency bias, solicited reviews |
| EnergySage | Verified quotes, real project data | Only shows installers in their network |
| BBB (Better Business Bureau) | Complaint history, resolution patterns | Low complaint volume can mean low market share, not quality |
| NABCEP Directory | Credential verification | Doesn't show reviews, but confirms certifications |
| Yelp | Older reviews, negative feedback | Aggressive filtering can bury legitimate complaints |
| Nextdoor / neighborhood Facebook groups | Hyperlocal, neighbor-verified | Small sample size |

Cross-reference is the key word here. An installer with 200 Google reviews and zero presence on Nextdoor in a suburb where they claim to have done 80 jobs deserves a second look. And always check whether the lead installer holds a [NABCEP certification](https://www.nabcep.org/). NABCEP (North American Board of Certified Energy Practitioners) is the gold standard for installer credentials. A company without any NABCEP-certified staff isn't necessarily bad, but it's a question worth asking.

## How to Verify What Reviews Are Telling You

Reading reviews is step one. Verifying them is step two. Here's a process that takes about 45 minutes and will tell you more than any number of stars.

**Step 1: Pull the contractor's license.** Every state has a contractor licensing database online. Look up the company's license number (they should provide it without hesitation), confirm it's active, and check for any disciplinary actions or complaints on file.

**Step 2: Call your local building department.** Ask whether the installer regularly pulls permits in your jurisdiction and whether they've had any issues with incomplete or non-compliant submittals. Building departments are often surprisingly forthcoming with this information.

**Step 3: Ask the installer for three references who are at least 12 months past their installation date.** A confident, reputable installer will have these ready. If they stall, offer only recent customers, or seem annoyed by the request, take note.

**Step 4: Ask those references specific questions.** Not just "did you like them?" Ask what system size they got, what annual production the company projected, and what they're actually seeing in their monitoring app. Ask whether anything went wrong and how it was handled. Ask whether they'd use the same company again and why.

**Step 5: Look up the installation address on Google Maps satellite view.** This sounds strange, but I've had clients do this and confirm the panels are actually installed. You can also see whether the system looks professionally done or haphazardly laid out.

**Step 6: Check for open permits.** Some jurisdictions let you search permit records online. An installer who has a cluster of permits that were opened but never closed (meaning the system was never inspected and signed off) is a serious red flag.

The [National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL)](https://www.nrel.gov/) has published research showing that installation quality directly affects long-term system performance and degradation rates. That's not abstract academic stuff. It means a poorly installed system produces less power over its 25-year life, and nobody's going to reimburse you for that lost production.

## Sizing Up the Sales Experience Before You Sign

The sales conversation itself is a review in progress. Pay attention to how the company handles your questions.

A trustworthy installer will use real data to size your system, specifically your last 12 months of utility bills. They should be able to show you a shading analysis using a tool like Aurora Solar or Helioscope, and they should explain their production estimate in terms of peak sun hours and system efficiency, not just "this system will cover 95% of your bill." Ask them what assumptions they're making. Ask what happens if production falls short.

Be wary of any installer who pressures you to sign at the first appointment, offers a "today only" discount, or can't clearly explain how their monitoring system works. A good monitoring setup, something like the SolarEdge mySolarEdge app or Enphase Enlighten, lets you track production in real time. If an installer is vague about what monitoring comes with the system, that's worth pressing on. ## Frequently Asked Questions

### How many reviews should a solar installer have before I trust them?

There's no magic number, but I'd want to see at least 40 to 50 reviews spread over two or more years before I put much weight in a rating. A company with 12 five-star reviews all posted within the same three-month period is likely running a post-installation review campaign, not organically earning that feedback.

### Should I only hire installers with NABCEP certification?

It's the strongest credential in the industry, and I'd prioritize it. That said, some excellent small regional installers have experienced electricians with years of solar work but haven't formalized the certification. In that case, verify their electrical license, check references carefully, and ask about their training process for new crew members.

### What if an installer has one or two really bad reviews mixed into mostly good ones?

Look at the substance of the negative reviews, not just the star rating. One complaint about a rude salesperson is different from one complaint about a roof leak that took nine months to fix. Also check whether the company responded and how. A single outlier with a professional response is not disqualifying. A cluster of complaints about the same specific issue, even if they're old, deserves your attention.

### Is it safe to hire a national solar company versus a local installer?

Both have real tradeoffs. National companies often have stronger financing options and warranties backed by larger balance sheets. But local installers tend to know the local permit offices, utility interconnection quirks, and roof types better. The worst-case scenario with a national company is that they go out of business or exit your market, leaving you without local support for warranty claims. Check whether the company has a physical office in your area, not just a sales team.

### Can I check if an installer's past permits were actually closed and inspected?

In many jurisdictions, yes. Search for your county or city's online permit portal and look up the contractor's name or license number. Open permits that were never finaled are a significant warning sign. It can mean the work didn't pass inspection or the contractor abandoned the job before getting sign-off. Your building department can tell you if a permit portal isn't available online.

Solar is a 25-year decision made in a few weeks, often with a contractor you've never met before. The reviews are out there, the license databases are public, and the references are a phone call away. Do the work upfront, and the system you end up with will be worth far more than the time you spent checking.
