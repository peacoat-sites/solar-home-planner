A homeowner in Phoenix signed a contract with a solar company in February, put down a $4,000 deposit, and by June the installer had gone out of business. The panels were never mounted. The deposit was gone. The lien the company had placed on her home during the financing process took three months and a lawyer to clear. This isn't a rare horror story. The solar industry has a contractor fraud problem, and it's getting worse as demand spikes and new, undercapitalized installers flood the market to chase the money.

Before you let anyone on your roof, you need to know what separates a legitimate solar contractor from one that's going to cost you time, money, and sleep.

## They Can't Answer Basic Questions About Your Permit

Every residential solar installation requires a permit. Full stop. If a salesperson tells you permits aren't needed in your area, or that they "handle all that behind the scenes," walk away immediately.

A legitimate installer will pull an electrical permit and, depending on your jurisdiction, a building permit as well. They'll submit plans to your local Authority Having Jurisdiction (AHJ) and get approval before a single panel goes on your roof. After installation, a city or county inspector comes out to sign off on the work. That inspection protects you, not the installer.

Ask the salesperson directly: "Who pulls the permit, and can I see a sample of the permit documentation you've submitted for past projects?" A real company can answer that without hesitation. They'll tell you the licensed electrician of record, explain the typical timeline in your jurisdiction, and confirm that your utility's interconnection application gets filed separately.

If they stumble, deflect, or tell you permits slow things down and they know how to work around it, that's not confidence. That's a red flag.

Also ask whether they're licensed in your state for both electrical and contractor work. Many states require a separate solar or low-voltage license. California, for example, requires a C-10 Electrical or C-46 Solar Contractor license. A company operating under just a general handyman license is not equipped to do this work legally.

I've seen this pattern more times than I can count. A salesperson shows up, spends 45 minutes at your kitchen table, and hands you a quote for a 7.2 kW system before they've looked at a single month of your electric bills or stepped foot in your attic.

Proper system sizing requires your last 12 months of utility bills to calculate your actual annual kilowatt-hour consumption. It requires a roof assessment, including the age of the shingles, the pitch, the orientation, and the available unshaded square footage. It requires a shading analysis, ideally using a tool like Solmetric SunEye or satellite-based software. And it requires an understanding of how your consumption might change, especially if you're adding an EV or planning a home addition.

The National Renewable Energy Laboratory has published research on how dramatically system performance varies based on local solar irradiance and shading conditions. A company that skips this analysis isn't saving you time. They're guessing. And if they guess too small, you'll still have a meaningful electric bill. If they guess too large, you've overpaid for capacity you'll never use.

Ask them specifically: "How did you arrive at this system size?" The answer should reference your actual kWh usage, your specific roof characteristics, and your local peak sun hours. If the answer is "that's what most people in your neighborhood get," find someone else.

## The Financing Terms Are Buried or Rushed

Solar financing deserves its own article, but the short version is this: the contract you sign matters enormously, and pressure to sign fast is always a red flag.

Legitimate installers give you time to read the paperwork. Predatory ones create artificial urgency. "This price is only good through Friday." "The federal tax credit is about to expire." (For the record, the federal Investment Tax Credit is currently 30% through 2032 under the Inflation Reduction Act. It's not about to expire.) "We only have two installation slots left this month." These are sales tactics, not facts.

If you're going with a loan product, read the interest rate, the loan term, and the total cost of financing. A 25-year solar loan at 7.99% APR on a $28,000 system will cost you significantly more than that system's sticker price by the time you're done. Run the actual numbers.

Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs) and leases are especially worth scrutinizing. You don't own the panels in those arrangements. The solar company does. That has implications for your home sale, your ability to claim the federal tax credit (you can't, the company takes it), and what happens if the company folds. The U.S. Department of Energy's homeowner solar guide specifically recommends that homeowners understand the difference between ownership, loans, leases, and PPAs before signing anything.

Watch for escalator clauses in PPAs, where the rate you pay for solar electricity increases by 2 to 3% per year. That sounds small until you're in year 15 paying more for solar power than you would have paid the utility.

## The Equipment They're Proposing Is Vague or Off-Brand

Not all solar panels are created equal. A reputable installer will tell you exactly which panels and inverters they're proposing, including the manufacturer, model number, rated wattage, and efficiency percentage. They'll also provide the warranty terms: typically a 25-year production warranty on panels and a 10 to 12-year warranty on string inverters, or 25 years on microinverters from manufacturers like Enphase.

Be cautious of vague proposals that just say "Tier 1 panels" without naming the manufacturer. "Tier 1" is a bankability rating from Bloomberg NEF, and it's been widely misused as a quality claim by salespeople. A company is Tier 1 if financial institutions consider them creditworthy enough to back large projects. It says nothing about panel efficiency, durability, or how well that manufacturer handles warranty claims.

Ask for the exact product spec sheets. Then look up whether that manufacturer has a U.S. warranty service presence. A panel with a 25-year warranty from a company with no U.S. service infrastructure is a warranty you'll have trouble ever collecting on.

Also ask who manufactures the racking system and what it's rated for. In high-wind or high-snow-load areas, racking specifications matter. Cheap imported racking that isn't rated for your local climate codes can fail, and when it does, the damage goes well beyond the panels.

## Step-by-Step: Vetting a Solar Installer Before You Sign

Here's a practical checklist you can run through with any company that makes it to your finalist stage.

**Step 1: Verify licenses.** Look up the company's contractor license number on your state licensing board's website. Confirm it's current and covers the work they're doing. Do the same for the individual electrician of record they list.

**Step 2: Check NABCEP certification.** The North American Board of Certified Energy Practitioners offers the gold standard credential in this industry. Ask if their lead installer is NABCEP PV Installation Professional certified. It's not required, but it signals serious professional investment.

**Step 3: Pull their permit history.** Many municipalities publish permit records online. Search for the company name and see how many permits they've pulled in the last 12 months and whether inspections passed on the first attempt. A pattern of failed inspections is a significant warning sign.

**Step 4: Call your utility.** Ask your utility's interconnection department if they're familiar with the company and whether the company submits complete interconnection applications. Utilities deal with bad installers regularly and are often surprisingly candid.

**Step 5: Read Google and BBB reviews specifically for complaints about post-install support.** Installation quality issues often show up six to 18 months after the job. Reviews from 2022 or 2023 will tell you more than fresh five-star ratings.

**Step 6: Confirm who does the work.** Ask whether the company uses their own employees or subs the installation out. Subcontracting isn't automatically bad, but you want to know who will actually be on your roof, and that the company takes responsibility for their work regardless.

**Step 7: Get three quotes.** Not to find the cheapest, but to compare scope, equipment, and what questions each company asks you. The best installer often isn't the lowest bidder. They're the one who asks the most questions.

| What to Ask | Good Answer | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| Who pulls the permit? | "We do, under our licensed electrician" | "We handle it, don't worry" |
| What panels are you proposing? | Specific manufacturer + model | "Tier 1 panels" |
| How did you size this system? | Based on 12 months of bills + shading analysis | "Industry standard for your area" |
| Can I see your license? | Provides number, you can verify it | Hesitates or deflects |
| What's your warranty process? | Clear point of contact, documented process | "Call the manufacturer" |

The solar industry has a lot of excellent, professional installers who do careful work and stand behind it for decades. They exist in every market. The problem is that the sales pressure, the financing complexity, and the influx of new companies chasing incentive money make it easy for the bad actors to blend in. You don't need to be suspicious of everyone. You just need to ask the right questions, take your time, and treat a $25,000 home improvement project with the same seriousness you'd give to any other major purchase.
