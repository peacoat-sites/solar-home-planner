A homeowner in Phoenix signed a contract with a solar company in February, put down $4,000, and by June the installer had vanished. The panels were never mounted. The deposit was gone. The lien the company had placed on her home took three months and a lawyer to clear. This isn't some obscure nightmare story buried in regulatory filings. The solar industry has a contractor fraud problem, and it's accelerating as demand spikes and undercapitalized outfits flood the market to chase easy money.

Before anyone touches your roof, you need to know what separates a real contractor from one that'll cost you cash, time, and sleep.

## They Can't Answer Basic Questions About Your Permit

Every residential solar installation requires a permit. That's non-negotiable. If a salesperson tells you permits aren't needed in your area, or that they "handle all that behind the scenes," leave.

A legitimate installer pulls an electrical permit and, depending on your jurisdiction, a building permit too. They submit plans to your local Authority Having Jurisdiction and get approval before a panel goes anywhere near your roof. After installation, a city or county inspector shows up to sign off. That inspection protects you, not the installer.

Ask directly: "Who pulls the permit, and can I see a sample from past projects?" A real company answers that immediately. They'll name the licensed electrician of record, explain the timeline in your area, and confirm the utility's interconnection application gets filed separately.

If they fumble, dodge, or claim permits just slow things down and they know workarounds, that's not confidence. It's a warning sign.

Also check whether they're licensed in your state for electrical and contractor work. Many states require a separate solar or low-voltage license. California mandates either a C-10 Electrical or C-46 Solar Contractor license. A company operating under a general handyman license isn't equipped for this job legally.

## The Quote Arrives Before Any Real Assessment

I've watched this happen dozens of times. A salesperson shows up, spends 45 minutes at your kitchen table, hands you a quote for a 7.2 kW system, and hasn't opened a single electric bill or looked in your attic.

Proper sizing needs your last 12 months of utility bills to calculate your actual annual consumption. It needs a roof assessment: shingle age, pitch, orientation, unshaded square footage. It needs shading analysis, ideally with something like Solmetric SunEye or satellite software. It needs understanding of how your consumption might shift if you're adding an EV or planning an addition.

The National Renewable Energy Laboratory has published research showing system performance varies dramatically based on local solar irradiance and shade. A company skipping this analysis isn't saving you time. They're guessing. Guess too small, you still have a fat electric bill. Guess too large, you've overpaid for capacity you'll never touch.

Ask: "How'd you get to this system size?" The answer should reference your actual kWh usage, your specific roof, and local peak sun hours. If they say "that's what most people in your neighborhood get," find someone else.

## The Financing Terms Are Buried or Rushed

Solar financing could be its own article. Short version: the contract you sign matters enormously, and pressure to sign fast is always a red flag.

Real installers give you time to read the paperwork. Predatory ones manufacture urgency. "This price expires Friday." "The federal tax credit's about to vanish." (The federal Investment Tax Credit is 30% through 2032 under the Inflation Reduction Act. It's not going anywhere.) "We only have two slots left this month." Sales tactics, not facts.

If you're taking a loan, read the interest rate, term, and total financing cost. A 25-year solar loan at 7.99% APR on a $28,000 system costs you significantly more than sticker price by the end. Run actual numbers.

Power Purchase Agreements and leases deserve extra scrutiny. You don't own the panels. The solar company does. That matters for your home sale, your ability to claim the federal tax credit (you can't, the company takes it), and what happens if they go under. The U.S. Department of Energy's homeowner solar guide specifically recommends understanding the difference between ownership, loans, leases, and PPAs before signing anything.

Watch for escalator clauses in PPAs. The rate you pay goes up 2 to 3% annually. Sounds tiny until year 15 when you're paying more for solar electricity than the utility would charge.

## The Equipment They're Proposing Is Vague or Off-Brand

Not all solar panels are equal. A reputable installer names exactly which panels and inverters they're proposing: manufacturer, model number, rated wattage, efficiency percentage. They'll provide warranty terms too, typically 25 years on panel production and 10 to 12 years on string inverters, or 25 years on microinverters from makers like Enphase.

Be wary of vague proposals saying "Tier 1 panels" without naming the manufacturer. "Tier 1" is a Bloomberg NEF bankability rating, and salespeople abuse it as a quality claim all the time. It means financial institutions consider them creditworthy for large projects. Nothing more. It says nothing about efficiency, durability, or warranty handling.

Ask for actual product spec sheets. Then check whether the manufacturer has U.S. warranty service infrastructure. A 25-year warranty from a company with no domestic support is a warranty you'll never collect on.

Also find out who makes the racking system and what it's rated for. In high-wind or heavy-snow areas, racking specs matter. Cheap imported racking unrated for your local climate codes can fail, and damage extends way beyond the panels.

## Step-by-Step: Vetting a Solar Installer Before You Sign

Run through this checklist with any finalist contractor.

**Step 1: Verify licenses.** Look up the contractor license number on your state licensing board's website. Confirm it's current and covers their work. Do the same for the electrician of record they list.

**Step 2: Check NABCEP certification.** The North American Board of Certified Energy Practitioners offers the gold standard credential. Ask if their lead installer holds NABCEP PV Installation Professional certification. Not required, but it signals serious professional commitment.

**Step 3: Pull their permit history.** Many municipalities publish permit records online. Search the company name and check how many permits they've pulled in the last 12 months and whether inspections passed first time. Failed inspections are a major warning sign.

**Step 4: Call your utility.** Contact the interconnection department and ask if they know the company and whether they submit complete applications. Utilities see bad installers constantly and are often surprisingly honest about them.

**Step 5: Read Google and BBB reviews for post-install complaints specifically.** Installation issues often surface six to 18 months after the job. Reviews from 2022 or 2023 tell you more than shiny new five-star ratings.

**Step 6: Confirm who does the work.** Ask whether the company uses employees or subs it out. Subcontracting isn't inherently bad, but you want to know who'll be on your roof and that the company owns responsibility for their work either way.

**Step 7: Get three quotes.** Not to find the cheapest, but to compare scope, equipment, and how many questions each company asks you. The best installer rarely is the lowest bidder. They're the one asking the most questions.

| What to Ask | Good Answer | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| Who pulls the permit? | "We do, under our licensed electrician" | "We handle it, don't worry" |
| What panels are you proposing? | Specific manufacturer + model | "Tier 1 panels" |
| How did you size this system? | Based on 12 months of bills + shading analysis | "Industry standard for your area" |
| Can I see your license? | Provides number, you can verify it | Hesitates or deflects |
| What's your warranty process? | Clear point of contact, documented process | "Call the manufacturer" |

The solar industry has plenty of excellent, professional installers who do meticulous work and stand behind it for decades. They're out there in every market. The problem is sales pressure, financing complexity, and new companies chasing incentive money make it trivial for bad actors to hide in plain sight. You don't need suspicion toward everyone. You just need to ask the right questions, move slowly, and treat a $25,000 home improvement the way you'd treat any major purchase. The Phoenix homeowner would tell you the same thing.
