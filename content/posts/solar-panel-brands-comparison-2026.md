Most people shopping for solar panels in 2026 spend way too much time comparing efficiency ratings and nowhere near enough time asking who's actually going to be around to honor that 25-year warranty. That's the thing nobody puts in the comparison chart.

I've been doing solar installs and consulting for long enough to have watched several "top-tier" brands quietly exit the US market, leaving homeowners with orphaned equipment and voided workmanship warranties. So before I get into who makes a good panel right now, I want to be honest: this market has shifted significantly even in the last 18 months, and some of the brand rankings you'll find from 2023 or 2024 are already outdated.

Here's where I actually started my research for this piece: [EnergySage's market data](https://news.energysage.com/) on installer preferences, combined with what I'm hearing from other installers in my network about what they're actually willing to put their name behind. Not just what the manufacturers are paying to promote.

## The Tier System Everyone Uses (And Its Real Limits)

You'll see "Tier 1" thrown around constantly in solar marketing. I'll be honest: it means less than you think. The Bloomberg NEF Tier 1 list is primarily a bankability rating, meaning it reflects whether a manufacturer has been used in projects that secured institutional financing. It tells you something about financial scale and production volume. It tells you almost nothing about actual panel quality, customer service, or whether that company will respond when you file a warranty claim in 2034.

What surprised me, when I started looking at this more carefully, is how many genuinely excellent panels come from manufacturers that aren't on the Tier 1 list, and how many mediocre panels carry that designation.

The more useful framework I use: look at the manufacturer's US market presence (do they have a domestic office and US-based warranty support?), their degradation rate (how much power they lose per year), their temperature coefficient (how much output drops in heat), and whether they carry both a product warranty and a separate power/performance warranty. Those last two are different things and a lot of homeowners don't realize it.

**REC Group** keeps earning its reputation. The REC Alpha Pure and Alpha Pure RX series pushed into the 430-445W range for residential panels, with a temperature coefficient around -0.24%/°C, which is genuinely impressive, especially for homeowners in hot climates like Arizona or Texas. REC has European roots but solid US distribution, and their 25-year product/performance warranty has held up well in the handful of claims I've seen processed. Not the cheapest option. Expect to pay a premium of roughly $0.10-0.15 per watt over a comparable JA Solar or Longi panel. Worth it for most installs? Probably yes, if your installer is comfortable with them.

**Maxeon Solar** (spun off from SunPower) makes panels I've consistently recommended for difficult roofs with limited space. Their Maxeon 7 series hits 24%+ efficiency in a 400W-ish package, which is the best real-world efficiency you'll find outside of some research-grade products. The catch: price. You're looking at a significant premium, and Maxeon has had some financial turbulence. Their warranty support has remained intact so far, but I'd be watching that situation carefully before signing a contract that locks you into Maxeon equipment long-term.

**Qcells (Hanwha Q CELLS)** is the brand I recommend most often to homeowners who want a strong balance of quality, price, and warranty confidence. Korean-owned (Hanwha), with a manufacturing facility in Dalton, Georgia, which matters for Buy American provisions and supply chain resilience. Their Q.PEAK DUO BLK ML-G10+ and newer G11 series panels offer solid 21-22% efficiency at prices that won't make your installer wince. The 25-year linear power warranty and product warranty are both strong, and Hanwha isn't going anywhere.

**Longi Solar (LONGi)** deserves more credit than it gets in US residential circles. They're the largest solar panel manufacturer in the world by volume, and their Hi-MO 6 and Hi-MO X6 series panels are genuinely competitive on efficiency (21-22.8% depending on configuration) at prices that make system economics much more attractive. The concern some installers raise is warranty claim logistics with a Chinese manufacturer. I think that's a legitimate but somewhat overstated worry for most residential systems, because the 25-year timeline means you're betting on corporate continuity regardless of who made the panel.

**Canadian Solar** sits in a similar position. Huge manufacturer, solid product, questions about long-term warranty support infrastructure in the US. Their HiDM 6 and BiHiKu7 series are technically strong. I've seen them installed on hundreds of houses without issues. For a budget-conscious homeowner whose primary goal is maximizing kWh per dollar, Canadian Solar is hard to argue against.

**SunPower** (distinct from Maxeon now) went through bankruptcy proceedings in 2024 and restructured significantly. This is the cautionary tale I mention to every client. SunPower was considered the gold standard for years. Their Maxeon cell technology was genuinely ahead of the market. And then the company imploded, leaving some dealers and homeowners in genuinely difficult positions mid-project. The panels themselves still work. But the warranty situation got complicated fast. The [Solar Energy Industries Association (SEIA)](https://www.seia.org/) has been pushing for better warranty protection standards partly because of situations like this one.

## What Actually Separates Good Panels From Great Ones

Efficiency is the number that gets marketed hardest, and it matters, but mostly in one specific situation: when you have limited roof space. If you've got a 2,000 square foot south-facing roof, the difference between a 21% and a 22% efficient panel is basically a rounding error in your final system size. If you've got a small east-west facing roof and need to squeeze maximum output from 12 panels, efficiency becomes the deciding factor.

The spec I don't see homeowners ask about enough: the first-year degradation figure. Most panels lose about 2-3% of output in year one, then degrade more slowly afterward. Some premium panels now advertise 1% or even 0.5% first-year degradation. Over a 25-year system life, that compounds into a meaningful difference in total energy production. REC and Maxeon have historically led here.

Temperature coefficient is the other underrated number. A panel rated at 400W at Standard Test Conditions (25°C, basically a mild lab day) produces noticeably less on a 95°F July afternoon. The coefficient tells you exactly how much: -0.30%/°C is average, -0.24%/°C is quite good, -0.20%/°C is excellent. If you're in Phoenix or Dallas or Atlanta, this number should matter to you more than the STC wattage.

One thing I'll tell you from experience: the best panel from a brand your installer has never worked with is often worse than a slightly lesser panel from a brand they've installed 300 times. Installers who know a product know where the fragile connectors are, how to read the monitoring software, and how to diagnose issues fast. Ask your installer which brands they have the most experience with before you insist on a specific product.

## The Honest Take on Made-in-USA Claims

There's been a lot of noise since the Inflation Reduction Act about domestic manufacturing. It's real, and it's worth caring about, but be specific. Qcells in Georgia is genuinely domestic manufacturing. Some other brands have assembly operations in the US but source cells and wafers almost entirely from Southeast Asia or China. "Assembled in America" and "manufactured in America" are very different claims. Ask your installer for the bill of materials if this matters to you.

The research here is mixed on whether domestically manufactured panels actually perform better or fail at lower rates. What's clearer is that domestic manufacturing tends to come with more accessible warranty support and less supply chain disruption risk. For a 25-year investment, that's not nothing.
