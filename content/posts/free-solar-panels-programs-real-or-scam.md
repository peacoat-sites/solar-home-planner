You've seen the ads. "Get solar panels installed on your home for FREE." Maybe it was a Facebook post, a mailer with a suspiciously professional logo, or a neighbor who swears she's getting solar at zero cost. And now you're sitting here wondering if you missed something, or if the whole thing is a trap.

Here's what I tell people who come to me with this question: the answer is genuinely both. Some "free solar" programs are completely legitimate. Others are borderline predatory. And the difference comes down to understanding what "free" actually means in each case, because the word is doing a lot of work in that headline.

Let me walk you through what's real, what's misleading, and how to tell the difference before you sign anything.

## What "Free Solar" Actually Means (Usually)

Nine times out of ten, when you see "free solar panels," the program in question is a solar lease or a Power Purchase Agreement, better known as a PPA. These are real financial products offered by legitimate companies like Sunrun, SunPower, and Tesla Energy. They're not scams. But they're also not free solar. Not even close.

Here's how they actually work: a solar company installs panels on your roof at no upfront cost to you. They own the equipment. You agree to either pay a fixed monthly lease fee, or you buy the electricity the panels generate at a set per-kilowatt-hour rate (that's the PPA model). Contracts typically run 20 to 25 years. The company gets to claim the federal tax credits and the depreciation benefits. You get cheaper electricity. Maybe.

I've seen PPA rates advertised at $0.09 to $0.13 per kilowatt-hour, which sounds great compared to what your utility charges. But that rate often includes an annual escalator clause of 2 to 3 percent, written into page six of a contract that's 30 pages long. Over 20 years, that compounds into something very different than what the door-to-door rep described.

So are solar leases and PPAs bad deals? Not always. If your credit score isn't high enough to qualify for a solar loan, or if you can't use the federal tax credit (more on that in a second), a lease can still reduce your electricity bill and get panels on your roof. For some households, it genuinely makes sense. But you need to go in with your eyes open, and "free solar panels" is not an honest description of the product.

There are legitimate assistance programs that get solar closer to truly free for qualifying households, and most people don't know they exist.

The federal Weatherization Assistance Program (WAP), administered by the Department of Energy, provides energy efficiency upgrades to low-income households and does sometimes include solar installations. The Income-Qualified Solar programs run by state utilities, especially in California, Colorado, and Massachusetts, can install solar at no cost to residents who meet income requirements. New York's NYSERDA has offered deep-discount solar programs for low-to-moderate income households that are worth looking up if you're in that state.

The 30 percent federal Investment Tax Credit (ITC) isn't "free" either, but it's the single most powerful solar incentive in the country right now, and it runs through 2032 at the current rate. A $20,000 system gets you a $6,000 reduction in your federal tax bill. The catch: you have to owe that much in federal taxes to claim it. If you're a retiree on a modest fixed income, this credit may do you very little good, which is one reason leases get pushed so hard to certain demographics.

Some electric cooperatives and municipal utilities also offer rebate programs that can cover $3,000 to $6,000 off an installation. The Database of State Incentives for Renewables and Efficiency (DSIRE) at dsireusa.org is the place to start if you want to see what's actually available in your zip code. That database is maintained with federal funding and it's free to use.

## The Actual Scams You Need to Watch For

Okay, here's where I want to be direct with you, because this is where real harm happens.

A reader emailed me last spring after signing what she thought was an enrollment form for a "federal solar grant program." It was not a grant application. It was a home improvement loan for $28,000, secured against her house, with a 9.9 percent interest rate. The installer had misrepresented the paperwork entirely.

That specific scheme, attaching a high-interest solar loan to a home's title through what's called a PACE loan (Property Assessed Clean Energy), has been the subject of regulatory action in multiple states. PACE financing is a real and sometimes useful product, but it's been systematically misused by bad actors who target elderly homeowners and Spanish-speaking communities in particular. The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau has documented this extensively.

Red flags I'd tell you to watch for:

- Any company that calls their product a "government grant" or "federal solar program" and can't name the actual program and the agency administering it. The federal government does not give out grants to individual homeowners for solar panels. That program does not exist.
- Door-to-door reps who pressure you to sign the same day. Legitimate solar companies quote you and give you time to compare. I've walked off more than a few job sites early in my career because a company was pushing customers to sign before they could read.
- Contractors who can't provide a license number, proof of insurance, and references on the spot. In most states, solar installation requires an electrical contractor's license. Ask for it.
- Anyone who tells you the panels will completely eliminate your electricity bill "guaranteed." System sizing doesn't work that way, and any honest installer will tell you production depends on weather, shading, roof angle, and your consumption patterns.
- Zero-interest loans that somehow have a "dealer fee" baked into the panel price, inflating the total system cost by 15 to 30 percent. This is more common than it should be. I've seen $15,000 systems quoted at $22,000 when financed through certain solar-specific lenders like Goodleap or Mosaic on terms set by shady installers.

The National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL) has published research on the gap between cash prices and financed prices for residential solar, and the numbers are genuinely alarming in some markets.

## How to Evaluate an Offer Without Getting Burned

Get at least three quotes. Always. Use the EnergySage marketplace if you want to do this online and compare multiple installers without fielding fifteen phone calls. The Solar Energy Industries Association (SEIA) maintains a contractor directory and a set of consumer protection principles that member companies agree to follow.

If someone offers you a lease or PPA, ask specifically: What is the monthly payment or per-kWh rate? Is there an escalator clause and if so what's the annual percentage? What happens when I sell my house (lease agreements transfer to buyers, which some real estate agents will tell you complicates sales)? What's the buyout price at year 5, 10, 15?

If someone offers you a loan, get the total cost of the loan, not just the monthly payment. A $25,000 system financed at 7.99 percent over 25 years costs you well over $50,000 by the time you're done. That math matters.

If someone tells you about a specific government program, ask for the program name and the government website, then look it up yourself. Don't let them navigate you there. Type the URL yourself.

For homeowners who are seriously considering going solar and want to understand their system before talking to any contractor, I'd recommend picking up a good solar DIY guide [like this one on Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=solar+installation+guide+homeowners) (the site may earn a commission on purchases) just to understand the vocabulary and the basics. You'll be a much harder person to mislead if you know what a string inverter is before a salesperson starts talking.

Home energy monitors like the Sense Energy Monitor or Emporia Vue are also worth considering before or alongside a solar installation, because understanding exactly where your electricity goes helps you right-size a system and catch inflated production estimates.

## FAQ

### Are there any truly free solar panel programs for homeowners?

Genuinely free solar, with no lease payments, no loan, and no PPA, exists primarily for income-qualified households through state utility programs and the federal Weatherization Assistance Program. If you meet income requirements, check your state's energy office website and look up programs through dsireusa.org. For most middle-income homeowners, "free" programs are actually leases or PPAs.

### Is the "30% government solar rebate" advertised online a scam?

The 30 percent federal Investment Tax Credit is real, but it's a tax credit, not a rebate or check in the mail. You claim it when you file your federal taxes, and it only helps you if you have enough federal tax liability to offset. Ads that call it a "rebate" or suggest you'll receive a cash payment are misleading, though the underlying credit is legitimate.

### Can a solar lease hurt me when I sell my home?

It can complicate things. Lease agreements typically have to be transferred to the new buyer or paid off at closing. Some buyers are reluctant to assume a 15-year lease obligation. It's not necessarily a dealbreaker, but it's a real consideration, and any installer who waves this off during the sales conversation is not being straight with you.

### How do I check if a solar contractor is licensed?

Every state has a contractor license lookup tool through the state licensing board, usually the Department of Consumer Affairs or the equivalent. Search "[your state] contractor license lookup" and you'll find it. In California that's the CSLB, in Florida it's the DBPR. Enter the company name or license number and verify it's active and has no serious complaints.

### What's the difference between a solar lease and a PPA?

With a lease, you pay a fixed monthly fee to rent the panels, regardless of how much electricity they produce. With a PPA, you pay for the actual electricity generated, at a set per-kilowatt-hour rate. PPAs can be a better deal in high-production months and worse in low-production months. Both leave the company owning the equipment and claiming the federal tax credit.
