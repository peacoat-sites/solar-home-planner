You've seen the ads. "Get solar panels installed on your home for FREE." Maybe it was a Facebook post, a mailer with a suspiciously professional logo, or a neighbor who swears she's getting solar at zero cost. And now you're sitting here wondering if you missed something, or if the whole thing is a trap.

Here's what I tell people who come to me with this question: the answer is genuinely both. Some "free solar" programs are completely legitimate. Others are borderline predatory. The difference comes down to understanding what "free" actually means in each case, because the word is doing a lot of work in that headline.

Let me walk you through what's real, what's misleading, and how to tell the difference before you sign anything.

## What "Free Solar" Actually Means (Usually)

Nine times out of ten, when you see "free solar panels," you're looking at a solar lease or a Power Purchase Agreement (PPA). These are real financial products from legitimate companies like Sunrun, SunPower, and Tesla Energy. Not scams. But also not free solar. Not even remotely.

Here's the actual mechanics: a solar company installs panels on your roof for nothing upfront. They own the equipment. You either pay a fixed monthly lease fee, or you buy the electricity the panels produce at a per-kilowatt-hour rate (the PPA model). Most contracts lock you in for 20 to 25 years. The company gets the federal tax credits and depreciation benefits. You get cheaper electricity. Theoretically.

I've seen PPA rates advertised at $0.09 to $0.13 per kilowatt-hour, which sounds good next to what your utility charges. But read page six of that 30-page contract. There's an annual escalator clause, usually 2 to 3 percent. Over 20 years that compounds into something drastically different from what the sales rep described over coffee.

Solar leases and PPAs aren't automatically bad deals. If your credit's not strong enough to qualify for a solar loan, or if you can't use the federal tax credit (more on this in a moment), a lease still cuts your electricity bill and gets panels on your roof. For certain households it genuinely makes sense. You just need to know what you're actually buying. "Free solar panels" is marketing language. That's all.

Legitimate assistance programs exist that get closer to truly free solar for qualifying households. Most people have no idea they're out there.

The federal Weatherization Assistance Program (WAP), run by the Department of Energy, provides energy efficiency upgrades to [low-income households](/solar-panels-for-low-income-homeowners-programs/). Solar installations sometimes get included. California, Colorado, and Massachusetts all run Income-Qualified Solar programs that can install panels at zero cost if you hit the income threshold. If you're in New York, NYSERDA has offered discounted solar for low-to-moderate income households worth checking out.

The 30 percent federal Investment Tax Credit (ITC) isn't "free" either, but it's the most powerful solar incentive in the country right now through 2032. Install a $20,000 system and get $6,000 knocked off your federal tax bill. The catch: you have to owe that much in taxes. A retiree on a fixed income might get almost nothing from this credit, which is why leases get pushed hard to that demographic.

Some electric cooperatives and municipal utilities offer rebates covering $3,000 to $6,000 of installation costs. Start at dsireusa.org, where the Database of State Incentives for Renewables and Efficiency keeps a free, federally-funded list of everything available in your zip code.

## The Actual Scams You Need to Watch For

A reader emailed me last spring. She'd signed what she thought was an enrollment form for a "federal solar grant program." It wasn't. It was a $28,000 home improvement loan, 9.9 percent interest, secured against her house. The installer had lied about what the paperwork was.

That scheme, attaching high-interest solar loans to your house through [PACE financing](/community-solar-how-it-works/) (Property Assessed Clean Energy), has triggered regulatory action in multiple states. PACE isn't inherently bad, but bad actors have weaponized it systematically against elderly homeowners and Spanish-speaking communities in particular. The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau has documented this extensively.

Red flags:

- Any company calling their product a "government grant" or "federal solar program" without naming the specific program and the agency running it. The federal government does not give grants to individual homeowners for residential solar. That program does not exist.
- Door-to-door reps pushing you to sign the same day. Legitimate installers quote you and let you think about it. I've walked off job sites early because a company was rushing customers into signatures before they could read the contract.
- Contractors who can't produce a license number, proof of insurance, and references immediately. Most states require an electrical contractor's license for solar work. Ask for it.
- Anyone guaranteeing the panels will completely eliminate your electricity bill. System sizing doesn't work that way. Weather, shade, roof angle, and your actual consumption all matter. Any honest installer will tell you this.
- Zero-interest loans where a "dealer fee" gets baked into the panel price, inflating total cost by 15 to 30 percent. I've seen $15,000 systems quoted at $22,000 through certain solar lenders like Goodleap or Mosaic when a sketchy installer controls the terms.

The National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL) published research on the spread between cash prices and financed prices for residential solar. In some markets the gap is alarming.

## How to Evaluate an Offer Without Getting Burned

Get at least three quotes. Always. Use EnergySage if you want to compare installers online without answering fifteen phone calls. The Solar Energy Industries Association (SEIA) has a contractor directory and consumer protection principles its members pledge to follow.

If someone offers a lease or PPA, ask specifics: What's the monthly payment or per-kWh rate? Is there an escalator clause, and if yes, what's the annual percentage? What happens when you sell your house (leases transfer to buyers, which real estate agents say complicates sales)? What's the buyout price at year 5, 10, 15?

If someone offers a loan, get the total loan cost, not just the monthly payment. A $25,000 system financed at 7.99 percent over 25 years costs well over $50,000 by the end. That math is important.

When someone mentions a government program, ask for the program name and government website, then look it up yourself. Don't let them navigate you there. Type the URL with your own hands.

If you're seriously considering solar and want to understand the basics before talking to any contractor, grab a good DIY guide [like this one on Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=solar+installation+guide+homeowners) (the site may earn a commission). You'll be much harder to mislead once you know what a string inverter is before a salesperson opens their mouth.

Energy monitors like the Sense Energy Monitor or Emporia Vue are worth considering before or alongside a solar installation. Understanding exactly where your electricity goes helps you right-size a system and catch inflated production numbers.

If you take one thing from this, let it be this: skepticism is not cynicism. Asking hard questions before you sign a 25-year contract isn't difficult. It's responsible. The right solar installer will welcome every single question.
