Maybe you've already priced out grid-tied solar and decided it's not for you. Maybe you're building a cabin 800 feet from the nearest utility pole, or you've just had your third multi-day outage this year and you're done depending on the grid. Whatever brought you here, you're asking one of the most consequential questions in all of residential solar: which battery is actually going to work for my situation? The answer isn't simple, and anyone who tells you it is probably wants to sell you something specific. Let's walk through this the right way.

## Why the Battery Is the Most Important Decision You'll Make

In a grid-tied system, the battery is optional. In an off-grid system, it's everything. Your panels produce power during daylight hours. Your refrigerator, well pump, and phone charger don't care about that schedule. The battery is the bridge between when energy is available and when you need it. Get the chemistry wrong, undersize the bank, or buy from the wrong supplier, and you'll learn exactly how critical that bridge is when it fails at 2 a.m. in January.

Off-grid battery selection comes down to five factors: chemistry (which determines cycle life and temperature performance), usable capacity (not just rated capacity), discharge rate, voltage compatibility with your inverter, and total cost over the system's lifetime. Most shoppers focus on upfront price and ignore everything else. That's how you end up replacing batteries every four years.

**Flooded Lead-Acid (FLA)**

This is the oldest technology and still widely used in off-grid systems. A well-maintained FLA battery bank, using Trojan T-105 or similar 6V golf cart batteries wired in series-parallel, can last 5 to 7 years with proper equalization charging. The upfront cost is the lowest of any option, typically $100 to $200 per battery. The tradeoffs are real: you need to add distilled water every one to three months, they vent hydrogen gas and need ventilation, and you can only safely use about 50% of rated capacity (discharging deeper accelerates plate sulfation and kills the bank early).

**AGM (Absorbed Glass Mat)**

AGM batteries are sealed, maintenance-free, and handle temperature swings better than flooded cells. They're a good fit for smaller weekend cabins or RV setups where simplicity matters and the system isn't running loads 24/7. Expect to spend $200 to $400 per 100Ah 12V battery. The cycle life at 50% depth of discharge (DoD) is typically 400 to 600 cycles, which is shorter than either FLA properly maintained or any lithium option. I've seen clients choose AGM because they liked the "no maintenance" pitch, then wonder why they're shopping for replacements after three years of full-time use.

**Lithium Iron Phosphate (LiFePO4)**

This is where the serious off-grid conversation is happening right now. LiFePO4 is thermally stable (it doesn't catch fire the way other lithium chemistries can), tolerates 80 to 100% depth of discharge, and delivers 2,000 to 6,000+ cycles depending on the brand and how you use them. The BMS (battery management system) built into quality units handles over-discharge protection, cell balancing, and temperature cutoffs automatically. A 100Ah 12V LiFePO4 battery typically runs $250 to $600, but when you calculate cost per usable kilowatt-hour over a 10-year lifespan, it frequently beats lead-acid. EnergySage's market data consistently shows LiFePO4 as the dominant chemistry in new residential storage installations, and that trend holds in off-grid builds, not just grid-tied backup systems.

**Nickel Iron (NiFe)**

These batteries deserve a mention because they have an almost legendary lifespan, sometimes 20 to 30 years with proper maintenance. They're the choice of serious homesteaders who want a system that outlasts everything else. The downsides: they're expensive, they have low efficiency (around 65 to 80% round-trip vs. 95%+ for LiFePO4), they require maintenance like FLA, and they charge and discharge slowly. For a typical off-grid home, NiFe is overkill in complexity and cost unless you have a very specific long-term build philosophy.

## How to Size Your Battery Bank for Off-Grid Use

Sizing is where most DIY off-grid systems go wrong. People either overbuild and spend too much, or undersize and spend the next decade frustrated by daily "low battery" warnings.

Here's a simple framework I use with clients:

**Step 1: Establish your daily energy consumption in watt-hours.**
Add up the wattage of every load and multiply by hours of use per day. A 60W refrigerator running 24 hours is 1,440Wh. A 1,500W water heater on a heat pump running 2 hours is 3,000Wh. Most full-time off-grid homes fall between 3,000 and 10,000Wh per day. The [National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL)](https://www.nrel.gov/) publishes detailed load analysis resources if you want to get precise.

**Step 2: Determine your days of autonomy.**
How many consecutive cloudy days do you need to survive without significant solar input? Two to three days is a reasonable target for most climates. If you're in a Pacific Northwest valley that sees week-long overcast stretches, plan for four to five.

**Step 3: Apply your depth of discharge.**
For LiFePO4, you can plan around 80% DoD safely. For lead-acid, use 50%.

**Step 4: Calculate total bank capacity.**

The formula: (Daily Wh x Days of Autonomy) / DoD = Required Wh of battery storage.

Example: 5,000Wh daily x 3 days / 0.80 DoD = 18,750Wh needed. At 48V nominal (the most efficient voltage for whole-home systems), that's approximately 390Ah of 48V battery capacity. A bank of eight 100Ah LiFePO4 batteries in a 48V configuration (requiring 4 batteries per string at 12V each) would cover this with headroom.

**Step 5: Cross-check your charge rate.**
Your solar array should be able to recharge the bank from 50% in one good solar day. If your bank is 20kWh and you can expect 5 peak sun hours, you need at least 2,000W of panels. Most off-grid systems benefit from slightly oversizing panels relative to the battery bank.

## Temperature, Installation, and Real-World Red Flags

Cold kills off-grid battery systems more reliably than any other single factor. Lead-acid batteries lose roughly 50% of their rated capacity at 32°F (0°C). LiFePO4 handles cold better but most units won't accept a charge below 32°F without a built-in self-heating feature. If you're in a climate that freezes, either buy a battery with a built-in heater (Ampere Time's heated models, or Battle Born's cold weather versions), or plan to house your battery bank in a conditioned or semi-conditioned space. A simple insulated battery enclosure with a small thermostat-controlled heater can solve this for a few hundred dollars.

Heat is a slower killer. LiFePO4 degrades faster above 95°F (35°C). Don't mount batteries in a south-facing metal shed in Arizona without ventilation and temperature control.

**Red flags to watch for when buying:**

Vague cycle life claims without specifying the DoD tested. "3,000 cycles" means nothing without knowing if that's at 80% DoD or 20% DoD.

No UL listing or IEC certifications. This matters for insurance and resale value, not just safety.

A seller who can't tell you the battery's continuous discharge rate (C-rate) and peak discharge rate. These determine whether the battery can actually power your loads.

No accessible BMS data or app integration. If you can't see cell voltage and temperature data, you're flying blind.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How long do off-grid solar batteries actually last?

Realistic lifespan depends heavily on chemistry and how you use them. Flooded lead-acid, properly maintained and cycled to 50% DoD, typically lasts 5 to 8 years. AGM is closer to 3 to 5 years under regular daily cycling. LiFePO4 from quality manufacturers can last 10 to 15 years in a well-designed system. Cycle life numbers on spec sheets assume controlled lab conditions. Real-world use, especially with temperature extremes or irregular charging, will reduce that.

### Can I mix old and new batteries in my bank?

You shouldn't. Mixing batteries of different ages, brands, or states of health creates imbalance in the bank. The weaker batteries drag down the stronger ones, accelerating degradation across the whole bank. If you need to expand capacity, it's usually better to create a separate new battery bank and wire it in parallel, keeping the old bank isolated as a secondary.

### What inverter do I need for an off-grid battery system?

You need a true inverter-charger, not just a grid-tie inverter. For whole-home off-grid systems, look at Victron Quattro or MultiPlus series, Sol-Ark 12K or 15K, or Schneider Electric XW+. These units manage battery charging from both solar (via a charge controller) and a backup generator, and they handle the power conversion from DC battery to AC household current. Make sure the inverter's battery voltage (12V, 24V, or 48V) matches your bank configuration.

### Is a generator still necessary with a battery bank?

For most full-time off-grid homes, yes. A generator is your backup for extended low-solar periods. The goal is to run it rarely, not constantly. A well-sized battery bank and solar array in a decent solar climate might mean running a generator only 5 to 10 times per year. I usually recommend sizing your battery bank for 3 days of autonomy and treating the generator as a fourth-day failsafe rather than a daily crutch.

### Do I need a permit to install an off-grid battery system?

In most U.S. jurisdictions, yes. Even off-grid systems typically require an electrical permit, and many areas require the installation to meet NEC Article 706 (for energy storage) and Article 690 (for solar). Some rural counties have minimal enforcement, but skipping permits creates problems with homeowner's insurance and property resale. If you're DIYing the install, check with your local building department before you start.

The honest truth about off-grid battery selection is that there's no perfect battery for every situation, only the right battery for your specific loads, climate, budget, and tolerance for maintenance. Do the load calculation before you shop. Size for realistic autonomy, not theoretical best-case solar production. And buy from a company that will answer technical questions before the sale, not just after something goes wrong. The off-grid life is genuinely achievable for most people who plan carefully. The battery bank you choose will determine whether that life feels like freedom or a part-time job.
