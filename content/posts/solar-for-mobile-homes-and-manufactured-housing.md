You own your land, you own your manufactured home, and your electric bill is running $180 a month. A neighbor down the road just got solar panels on their site-built house and cut their bill in half. So why does every solar company you call either quote you a sky-high price, tell you it's "complicated," or just stop returning your calls? That's not bad luck. That's a real pattern, and it has everything to do with how the industry views manufactured housing, not with whether solar actually works for your situation. It does. You just need to know the rules before you start.

## Why Solar Companies Hesitate on Manufactured Homes (and Why That's Their Problem)

Let me be blunt about something I've seen repeated constantly in this industry: most residential solar installers are optimized for stick-built homes with composition shingle roofs, standard attic access, and 200-amp service panels. The moment you introduce a manufactured home, they're suddenly out of their comfort zone, and instead of saying that, some will give you vague excuses or inflated quotes to make you go away.

The actual concerns are legitimate but manageable. They include roof load capacity, wind zone ratings, chassis grounding requirements, and whether your home is on a permanent foundation. None of these are deal-breakers if you understand them.

Here's the core issue with roof-mounted panels on a manufactured home: the roof structure is typically lighter than a site-built home. Standard rafter spacing might be 16 or 24 inches on center, but the rafters themselves are often 2x3 or 2x4 lumber rather than the 2x6 or 2x8 you'd find in conventional construction. Attaching racking hardware to an undersized rafter with improper load calculations is a structural problem. It's not a solar problem.

A competent installer will pull your home's data plate (required by the HUD code to be inside every manufactured home, usually in a cabinet or near the electrical panel), confirm your wind zone and roof load rating, and design accordingly. If they don't ask about the data plate, that's a red flag worth taking seriously.

## Ground Mounts: Often the Better Option for Manufactured Housing

When a roof mount is complicated, a ground mount is often cleaner, cheaper in the long run, and more efficient. I recommend homeowners who own their lot seriously evaluate ground mounting before defaulting to a rooftop system.

Ground-mounted arrays sit on steel posts driven or cemented into the ground at a fixed or adjustable tilt angle. For manufactured housing, this solves the structural load question entirely. Your roof carries nothing. The panels face whatever direction gives you the best production, typically south-facing at a tilt roughly equal to your latitude.

The tradeoffs are real too. You need adequate yard space, generally around 100 square feet per kilowatt of capacity for a fixed-tilt system. You'll run a conduit from the array to your home's electrical panel, which means trenching. In most jurisdictions, that conduit needs to be buried at least 18 inches deep (24 inches for direct-burial cable without conduit, per NEC 300.5). Permits are required. Don't let anyone tell you they can skip the permit on a ground mount.

On the cost side, expect ground mount hardware to add roughly $0.20 to $0.50 per watt compared to a simple roof mount, but you often recover that in better panel orientation and lower maintenance costs. Ground-mounted panels are also dramatically easier to clean, which matters for production over a 25-year system life. If you want to maintain performance yourself, a [solar panel cleaning kit with a soft-bristle brush and purified water attachment](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=solar+panel+cleaning+kit) (affiliate link, site may earn a commission) will cost you $30 to $60 and pay for itself in the first year.

## System Sizing for Manufactured Homes: Start With Your Actual Usage

Here's where a lot of homeowners get misled. A salesperson will look at your address, assume you need a 6kW system, and quote accordingly. But manufactured homes vary enormously in energy use depending on size, age, insulation quality, and what kind of HVAC you're running.

Pull your last 12 months of utility bills and add up your total kilowatt-hour consumption. Don't just look at the dollar amount; look at the kWh number on each statement. Divide the annual total by 365 to get your daily average. That's your baseline.

For a 1,000 square foot double-wide in the Southeast with a heat pump, you might use 900 to 1,100 kWh per month in summer. A well-insulated single-wide in the Pacific Northwest with minimal AC might use 350 to 500 kWh. These require completely different system sizes.

The [National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL)](https://www.nrel.gov/) publishes free solar irradiance data through their PVWatts Calculator, which lets you enter your exact address, your system size, panel tilt, and azimuth, and get a realistic annual production estimate. Use it. It's what your installer should be using, and you can verify their numbers yourself before signing anything.

A rough sizing formula: take your monthly kWh usage, divide by 30 (days), divide by your location's peak sun hours (4 to 6 for most of the continental U.S.), and that gives you approximate system wattage needed. A home using 900 kWh/month in a location with 5 peak sun hours needs about 6,000 watts, or 6kW, of panels.

## Permits, Codes, and HUD Compliance: What's Actually Required

This is where manufactured housing diverges most sharply from conventional homes, and getting it wrong can affect your insurance, your financing, and your ability to sell the property.

Manufactured homes built after June 15, 1976 are governed by the HUD Manufactured Home Construction and Safety Standards. Solar installations on HUD-code homes are considered "alterations" and must comply with those standards in addition to local electrical codes. Some states have additional oversight through a state administrative agency (SAA). California, for example, has specific CalHCD requirements for alterations to manufactured homes.

What this means practically: your permit application may need to go to both the local building department and the state SAA, depending on where you live. Your installer needs to know this. Many don't, especially smaller companies new to manufactured housing.

For electrical work specifically, the home's original wiring is typically done to the NEC as adopted by HUD at the time of manufacture. If your home was built in 1995, it was wired to the 1993 NEC. Your new solar installation will be inspected under your current local code adoption, which may be NEC 2020 or 2023. A good inspector will reconcile these, but expect some questions about your existing panel and whether it can safely backfeed from a solar inverter.

The [U.S. Department of Energy's homeowner guide to going solar](https://www.energy.gov/eere/solar/homeowners-guide-going-solar) outlines the permitting basics that apply nationally. Read it before you talk to any contractor. Walking into a sales call knowing the difference between a building permit and an interconnection agreement tells the salesperson immediately that you're not going to be an easy upsell.

One more point on grounding: manufactured homes use a chassis ground. The home's electrical system bonds to the steel frame. This is different from a site-built home with a ground rod system. Your solar installer's grounding scheme needs to account for this, especially on older homes where the chassis bond may have corroded or been modified.

## Financing Solar on a Manufactured Home

Getting a loan for solar on a manufactured home is harder than it should be. Here's why: most solar loan products are tied to home equity or require the home to be classified as real property. Many manufactured homes, even when owner-occupied, are titled as personal property (like a vehicle) rather than real estate, especially if they're on a rented lot.

If your home is on a permanent foundation and you've converted the title to real property, you may qualify for a home equity loan, a HELOC, or a Title I FHA loan for energy improvements. If it's still titled as personal property, your options narrow significantly. You're looking at personal loans, which carry higher interest rates, or manufacturer/installer financing, which should be scrutinized closely.

The 30% federal solar Investment Tax Credit (ITC) applies regardless of home type, as long as you own the system and have federal tax liability to offset. If you're buying through a lease or PPA (Power Purchase Agreement), you don't own the panels and you don't get the credit. For manufactured homeowners on tight budgets, the distinction between owning and leasing your system is financially significant.

Some states and utilities offer additional incentives, rebates, or on-bill financing programs. The Database of State Incentives for Renewables and Efficiency (DSIRE) at dsireusa.org is the go-to resource. Check it before assuming your only option is a cash purchase or a retail installment loan at 9.99%.

## Step-by-Step: Evaluating Whether Solar Makes Sense for Your Situation

Use this sequence before you talk to a single contractor. It'll save you time and protect you from making decisions under pressure.

**Step 1: Confirm your property ownership situation.** Do you own the land? Is the home on a permanent foundation? Is the title real property or personal property? These answers determine your financing options and permitting path.

**Step 2: Pull your data plate.** Locate the HUD data plate inside your home. Note the wind zone (Zone I, II, or III), the roof live load rating (usually 20, 30, or 40 psf), and the thermal zone. Photograph it. You'll share this with every installer you talk to.

**Step 3: Calculate your actual energy use.** Gather 12 months of utility bills. Total the kWh, not the dollars. Calculate your monthly average. Run PVWatts with your address to understand your solar resource.

**Step 4: Decide roof mount vs. ground mount.** Consider roof age (if your roof is under 10 years of life expectancy, plan to replace before adding panels), available south-facing yard space, and shading from trees or structures.

**Step 5: Get at least three quotes.** Ask each installer specifically: "Have you permitted solar installations on HUD-code manufactured homes in this state before?" If they hesitate, that's your answer.

**Step 6: Review the interconnection agreement.** Your utility will require you to sign an interconnection agreement before you can go live. Read it. Understand net metering terms, export limits, and any fees for grid-tied systems.

**Step 7: Consider adding a home energy monitor.** Before or shortly after installation, install a [whole-home energy monitor](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=home+energy+monitor) (affiliate link, site may earn a commission) like the Emporia Vue or Sense. Knowing exactly where your electricity goes helps you make better decisions about battery storage, appliance upgrades, and load management.

## FAQ

### Can I put solar on a manufactured home that's in a mobile home park?

Yes, but it's more complicated. If you don't own the land, a roof mount may be your only option since ground mounts require land rights. You'll also need the park owner's permission, and many parks have lease agreements that restrict exterior modifications. Review your lease carefully before spending money on a site evaluation. Some parks have blanket policies against solar. Others are fine with it. Get any approval in writing.

### Does the federal solar tax credit apply to manufactured homes?

Yes. The 30% ITC applies to solar installations on your primary residence regardless of whether it's a manufactured home or site-built. The home must be in the U.S., and you must own (not lease) the solar system. The credit is claimed on IRS Form 5695. If your tax liability in year one is less than the full credit, the remaining balance carries forward to the following tax year.

### What happens if I need to re-roof while I have solar panels?

You'll need to have the panels temporarily removed and reinstalled after the roof work is done. This is called a "pull and reset" and typically costs $500 to $1,500 depending on system size and your location. If your manufactured home's roof is aging, address it before installing solar. Adding panels to a roof that needs replacement in three years is an expensive mistake.

### Can I add battery storage to a manufactured home solar system?

Absolutely. Battery storage is especially valuable for manufactured home owners in areas with unreliable grid power or time-of-use utility rates. Systems like the Tesla Powerwall or Enphase IQ Battery can be wall-mounted inside or outside the home. Verify your utility's interconnection requirements for battery-paired systems, as some utilities have additional requirements or restrictions on battery backup modes.

### Is a DIY solar installation legal on a manufactured home?

In some jurisdictions, yes, but with significant caveats. The HUD alteration requirements mean any modification to a manufactured home needs to meet federal standards, and a permit is almost certainly required. Some states allow licensed homeowners to pull their own electrical permits; others don't. The electrical work on a solar installation involves working with both DC and AC systems, inverter wiring, and utility interconnection. If you're not a licensed electrician and comfortable with that work, hire out the electrical portion even if you handle the mounting and wiring runs yourself.

You've already done the hard work of owning your home. Adding solar is just the next smart step.
