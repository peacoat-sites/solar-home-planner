Imagine you've parked your 200-square-foot tiny home on a piece of land in rural Tennessee, the power company wants $18,000 to run a line to your property, and your only alternative is a gas generator that burns through $200 a month in fuel. That's not a hypothetical. I've talked to tiny home owners in exactly that situation who went solar, recouped their install costs in under three years, and never looked back. If you're building or already living in a tiny home, DIY solar isn't just a fun project. It might be the most financially sensible decision you make about your entire build.

## Why Tiny Homes Are Actually Ideal for Solar

Here's the thing most solar salespeople won't tell you: the smaller your load, the easier and cheaper it is to go solar. A conventional 2,000-square-foot house might consume 10,000 to 14,000 kWh per year. A well-built tiny home? Often under 2,000 kWh annually, sometimes as low as 1,200 kWh if you've swapped out energy hogs for efficient alternatives.

That smaller load translates directly to a smaller, cheaper system. You're not looking at a 20-panel, 8 kW rooftop array with a $30,000 price tag. A typical off-grid tiny home setup runs somewhere between 800W and 2,400W of solar panels paired with 100 to 300 amp-hours of lithium battery storage. The whole thing, done DIY, might cost you $3,000 to $7,000 depending on battery chemistry and panel brand.

The National Renewable Energy Laboratory has published extensive data showing that small-load off-grid systems have some of the fastest payback periods in residential solar, especially when you factor in avoided grid connection costs. For rural tiny home owners, that math is almost always a slam dunk.

The roof situation on a tiny home does create some constraints, though. Most tiny homes on wheels (THOWs) have metal roofs, which are actually excellent for solar, both structurally and thermally. If your home is on a foundation, you have more flexibility, including ground-mount options that let you track the sun more effectively. You can read more about [how your roof type affects your solar options](/best-roof-type-for-solar-panels/) before you commit to a mounting strategy.

This is where a lot of first-timers go wrong. They buy a "400W solar kit" they saw on Amazon and wonder why their batteries are dead by 9 PM. Sizing is a two-part problem: how much power do you need daily, and how many days of cloudy weather do you need to survive without the sun.

Start with a load audit. Go appliance by appliance.

| Appliance | Watts | Hours/Day | Daily Wh |
|---|---|---|---|
| LED lighting (6 bulbs) | 60W total | 4 hrs | 240 Wh |
| Laptop | 65W | 6 hrs | 390 Wh |
| Mini-split (efficient 9,000 BTU) | 750W | 4 hrs | 3,000 Wh |
| Refrigerator (12V compressor) | 45W avg | 24 hrs | 1,080 Wh |
| Phone/device charging | 30W | 3 hrs | 90 Wh |
| Water pump | 60W | 0.5 hrs | 30 Wh |
| **Daily Total** | | | **~4,830 Wh** |

That example system needs roughly 5 kWh per day. If you're in a location with an average of 5 peak sun hours (check NREL's PVWatts calculator for your exact location), you need at least 1,000W of panels just to break even on a perfect sunny day. Add 25 to 30 percent for real-world inefficiencies like heat, wiring losses, and inverter conversion. You're probably looking at 1,300W to 1,500W of solar capacity.

For battery sizing, the standard off-grid recommendation is 2 to 3 days of autonomy. At 5 kWh/day, that's 10 to 15 kWh of usable storage. With lithium iron phosphate (LiFePO4) batteries, which can be discharged to 80 to 100 percent depth, you'd want 12.5 to 19 kWh of rated capacity. That's a meaningful investment. If budget is tight, start with one day of autonomy and a generator for backup. Be honest with yourself about your actual usage before you buy.

A home energy monitor like the Emporia Vue or Sense can give you real consumption data from day one. If you're already living in the space with grid or generator power, [monitoring your actual usage](https://amzn.to/emporia-vue) (Amazon, commissions may apply) for two to four weeks before buying components will save you from costly oversizing or undersizing.

## Choosing Your Components: Panels, Batteries, and Inverters

You've got four main components to select: solar panels, a charge controller, a battery bank, and an inverter (or inverter-charger combo).

**Panels:** For a tiny home roof, you'll likely be working with 200W to 400W monocrystalline panels. Mono panels are worth the slight cost premium over polycrystalline because they perform better in low light and generate more power per square foot. A 200-square-foot THOW roof might physically fit 4 to 6 panels depending on pitch and obstructions. Measure twice.

**Charge controller:** MPPT (Maximum Power Point Tracking) controllers are the standard recommendation for systems larger than about 400W. They're significantly more efficient than PWM controllers, especially in cold weather and variable light. Victron Energy makes excellent MPPT controllers that communicate with other system components. A 40A or 60A Victron SmartSolar is a reasonable choice for most tiny home builds.

**Batteries:** LiFePO4 is now affordable enough that I'd rarely recommend lead-acid for a new build. Brands like Battle Born, Renogy, and EG4 offer quality cells with solid BMS (battery management system) protection. Yes, LiFePO4 costs more upfront, but the cycle life difference is enormous. A good LiFePO4 pack delivers 3,000 to 5,000 cycles. Lead-acid, maybe 500.

**Inverter:** For a tiny home running standard 120V appliances, a pure sine wave inverter is non-negotiable. Modified sine wave inverters will damage or degrade sensitive electronics, including many modern appliance motor controllers. If you want the ability to charge batteries from shore power or a generator, look at an inverter-charger combo unit from Victron (Multiplus series) or Schneider Electric (XW+ series).

## Installation: What You Can Do Yourself and What You Shouldn't

I'll be direct here. A lot of the wiring work on a tiny home solar system falls into what most jurisdictions consider low-voltage or self-contained electrical work, especially if it's a vehicle-titled THOW that doesn't require a building permit. That creates genuine DIY opportunity. But don't let that freedom make you sloppy.

Here's a reasonable step-by-step for a DIY off-grid tiny home solar install:

**Step 1: Mount the panels.** Use rail mounts or Z-brackets on a metal roof with proper lap sealant or EPDM tape. Don't trust silicone alone on a standing seam roof. Every roof penetration is a potential leak. Aluminum mounting rails from Unistrut or dedicated solar mounting companies give you flexibility.

**Step 2: Run the DC wiring.** Use UV-resistant USE-2 or PV wire for any outdoor runs. Properly size your wire based on amperage and run length to keep voltage drop below 2 percent. Use appropriately rated MC4 connectors, and never mix brands without a cross-compatibility check.

**Step 3: Install the charge controller.** Mount it near the batteries in a ventilated area. Wire solar input first (with breaker), then battery connection. Follow the manufacturer's sequence exactly.

**Step 4: Install the battery bank.** LiFePO4 batteries must be protected from freezing temperatures during charging. If your tiny home gets below 32°F regularly, you need either a battery with a built-in heating element or a heated battery compartment.

**Step 5: Install the inverter.** Keep the cable run from battery to inverter as short as possible, ideally under 3 feet. Use appropriately sized lugs and proper torque specs. This connection carries the highest amperage in your whole system.

**Step 6: Test and commission.** Verify polarity before connecting batteries. Use a clamp meter to confirm charging current from panels. Monitor for heat at connections during the first few charge cycles.

## Permits, Regulations, and the HOA Problem

This is where tiny home solar gets complicated. If your tiny home is THOW (titled as a vehicle), you typically operate in a regulatory gray zone. Most building departments don't inspect vehicle electrical systems, and your solar install may be considered part of the vehicle's 12V or off-grid system. But the moment you connect to grid-tied shore power at an RV park or campground, different rules can apply.

If your tiny home is on a foundation, you're subject to the same permit requirements as any residential structure. That means a proper permit, possibly an electrical inspection, and compliance with your local version of the National Electrical Code. Don't skip permits on foundation-mounted installs. Unpermitted electrical work can void your homeowner's insurance and create problems when you sell.

If you're in an HOA community, the rules get thornier. The Solar Energy Industries Association notes that over 20 states have solar access laws that limit HOA authority to ban solar, but those laws have significant variation in scope and enforcement. Some HOAs can still dictate panel placement, aesthetics, and installation standards even where bans are prohibited. Before you buy anything, review your HOA's CC&Rs and understand your state's solar rights statute. The full breakdown of [HOA solar panel rules](/hoa-solar-panel-rules/) covers this state by state.
