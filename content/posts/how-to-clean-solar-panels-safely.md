Most people wait too long to clean their solar panels. Then they finally do it wrong and either scratch the glass or void a warranty they didn't know they had.

You're probably here because your bill crept back up, or you noticed the panels look grimy, or someone told you cleaning them would make a big difference. Maybe you're just being thorough. All of those are good reasons to pay attention to this, because dirty panels are a real performance problem, not just a cosmetic one. Depending on where you live, soiling losses can run anywhere from 1.5% to over 25% of annual production, according to research from the [National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL)](https://www.nrel.gov/). In a dusty inland area of California or anywhere in the Southwest, that's not a rounding error.

Here's what I tell people who ask me about cleaning: it's genuinely simple, but the simple version requires knowing a few specific things first. Skip those, and you risk cracking a panel, getting zapped, or triggering a manufacturer warranty dispute you'd rather not have.

## Before You Touch Anything: Safety and Timing

The single most important thing isn't what soap to use. It's when and how you get up there.

Never clean panels in the middle of a hot day. Glass that's been sitting in direct sun for six hours can be 90°F or more above ambient temperature. Spraying cold water on hot glass creates thermal stress. I've never personally seen a panel crack from this, but I've talked to installers who have, and panel manufacturers specifically warn against it. Early morning is ideal: the panels are cool, dew is already doing some pre-soaking work for you, and you're not fighting glare. Late afternoon after the sun drops works too.

Turn off your solar system before you start. This is non-negotiable. On most modern inverters, there's a DC isolator or a solar supply main switch, often right at the inverter box. Some systems have a shutdown procedure. Check your inverter manual. You're not just protecting yourself from electrical current on the roof; you're also preventing a ground fault if water gets into any compromised conduit or junction box. If you have an Enphase microinverter system, use the Rapid Shutdown switch if your system has one.

Roof safety matters more than panel cleaning. If your panels are on a steep-pitch roof, a second story, or you're not comfortable on ladders, hire someone. I'll tell you what to look for when you do. But please don't convince yourself you're more sure-footed than you are. Falls from roofs are not a DIY learning experience you recover from.

If your panels are on a low-slope or ground-mount system, most of the process can be done from the ground or a stepstool, which makes everything much easier and safer.

You don't need a special solar panel cleaning solution. There. I said it. Most of what's marketed specifically for solar panels is either regular dish soap rebottled at four times the price, or deionized water, which is genuinely useful but not required for most homeowners.

Here's what actually works:

A soft-bristle brush or a squeegee with a long handle, warm water, and a tiny amount of mild dish soap (we're talking a few drops of Dawn in a bucket). That's the core kit. If you want to step up slightly, a [solar panel cleaning kit](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=solar+panel+cleaning+kit&tag=solarsite-20) that includes a microfiber head on an extendable pole lets you reach panels from the roof edge or from the ground on a ground mount without walking across the glass. What you should not use: abrasive scrubbers, steel wool, anything with ammonia (Windex), pressure washers, or any cleaning product that contains fragrance or wax. Pressure washers can crack the glass, damage the frame seals, and drive water into places it should never go. I've watched a homeowner pressure-wash his panels and walk away feeling great about it. Six months later his inverter was throwing ground fault errors because water had gotten into a conduit penetration the pressure had forced open. Don't do it.

Hard water is a sneaky problem. If your tap water is hard (high mineral content), letting it air-dry on the panels will leave mineral deposits that are actually harder to remove than the original dirt. Either dry the panels immediately with a squeegee, use a water filter on your hose (a simple inline carbon block filter helps), or in hard-water areas, pick up a gallon or two of deionized water from an auto parts store for the final rinse. It's about $1.50 a gallon at AutoZone and it makes a noticeable difference in the finish.

## The Actual Cleaning Process

This is where people overcomplicate things.

Start by rinsing the panels with plain water from your garden hose. A gentle shower setting, not a jet. This removes loose dust, bird droppings that haven't fully dried, and pollen before you start scrubbing. If you scrub first, you're just dragging grit across the glass.

Next, with your soft brush or microfiber pad, work in sections. Wet the brush, apply a small amount of soapy water, and scrub gently in straight lines rather than circles. You're not trying to polish the glass; you're lifting particulate and organic residue. Panels on low-tilt roofs collect more standing dirt because rain doesn't wash them as effectively, so you may need to go over those spots twice.

Bird droppings deserve special attention. Dried bird droppings are one of the worst soiling culprits because they cause "hotspot" shading, where a small opaque spot creates disproportionate output loss in a cell string. Don't scrape them. Soak them first with a wet cloth or wet paper towels laid directly on the deposit for a few minutes. Once softened, they come off easily with light pressure. Forcing them off dry can scratch the anti-reflective coating.

Rinse thoroughly after washing. Any soap residue left on the glass will attract dirt faster and reduce light transmission slightly. If you're using deionized water, do your final rinse with that.

Dry with a squeegee if you're on a flat or low-slope surface. On a steep-pitch roof, a squeegee is awkward and the panels will air-dry quickly anyway, especially in the morning once the sun comes up. If you're using hard tap water and can't squeegee, mop up what you can with a clean microfiber cloth.

The whole process on an average 20-panel residential system should take 45 minutes to an hour.

## How Often Do You Actually Need to Do This?

Honestly, the answer varies by location more than almost any other factor.

In the Pacific Northwest, where rain is frequent and dust is minimal, panels can go a full year without meaningful cleaning. In Phoenix, Albuquerque, or the Central Valley of California, you might clean four times a year and still notice a difference between cleanings. [EnergySage's market data](https://news.energysage.com/) suggests most homeowners in average U.S. climates see meaningful performance recovery by cleaning once or twice a year, typically in late spring after pollen season and again in late fall before the lower-angle winter sun matters most.

Pollen season is the one that catches people off guard. A yellowish haze on the glass is almost pure pollen, and it's a worse light blocker than plain dust because of how it coats the anti-reflective coating at a microscopic level. If you live somewhere with heavy spring pollen, add a cleaning right after peak pollen season, usually late April or May depending on your region.

One useful trick: pull up your monitoring app (Enphase Enlighten, SolarEdge, or whatever your inverter uses) and compare current daily output to the same period last year. If production is down 8-12% on clear days and you haven't had a cleaning in six months, that's a cleaning problem. If it's down that much and you cleaned last month, it might be something else, like a failing microinverter or shading from a tree that's grown in.

## If You're Hiring Someone

A professional cleaning for a typical residential system runs $150-$300 depending on system size, roof pitch, and your area. That's a reasonable price once a year. It's not a reasonable price every month, so be skeptical of any company pushing quarterly "maintenance contracts" unless you're in an extremely high-soiling environment.

Red flags: anyone using a pressure washer, anyone who doesn't turn off the system first, anyone who can't tell you the brand of brush or cleaning agent they're using. Ask those questions before they get on your roof.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I clean solar panels with a pressure washer?

No. Even at a moderate setting, pressure washers can crack the tempered glass, damage the rubber frame seals, and force water into conduit penetrations or junction boxes. Manufacturers explicitly void warranties for damage caused by pressure washing, and that kind of water intrusion can cause intermittent faults months after the fact.

### Will cleaning my panels actually make a noticeable difference in my electric bill?

Usually yes, especially if it's been over a year or you live in a dusty or high-pollen area. NREL data shows soiling losses averaging 1.5-6% annually in typical U.S. locations, with much higher numbers in arid climates. That can translate to $50-$150 a year in lost production on a mid-size residential system, so one cleaning pays for itself easily.

### Do I need to turn off my solar system before cleaning?

Yes. Shut down via the DC isolator or the solar supply main switch at the inverter before getting on the roof or directing water anywhere near the system. This is both a safety measure and a warranty protection step.

### Is rainwater enough to keep panels clean on its own?

In rainy climates with moderate tilt, rain handles a lot of the routine dust. But rain doesn't do much for bird droppings, pollen, or heavy soiling, and flat-mount panels don't self-clean well at all. Think of rain as maintenance between cleanings, not a substitute.

### What's the best time of day to clean solar panels?

Early morning, before the panels heat up in direct sun. Cleaning hot glass with cool water creates thermal stress and can cause micro-cracking over time. Early morning also means dew has been softening surface dirt, and you'll be done before the day heats up.
